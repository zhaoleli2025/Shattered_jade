## Battle Brothers — tactical combat rules (wiki digest)

SUMMARY: The Battle Brothers wiki documents a hex-grid, initiative-ordered tactical combat system built on two parallel per-action resources (9 Action Points + accumulating Fatigue), a two-layer damage model (head/body armor durability over hitpoints, with ~10% of remaining armor mitigating bleed-through damage), and a transparent additive/multiplicative hit-chance formula (skill − defense, clamped 5–95%, with diminishing returns on defense above 50). Around this core sit interacting subsystems: a six-state morale ladder driven by Resolve-based checks with quantified event modifiers, head hits as the crit system (25% base, +50% HP damage), Zone of Control with free attacks that stop movement on hit, surround bonuses implemented as defense reduction, elevation granting ±10% hit chance per level, and a temporary/permanent injury layer keyed to damage as a fraction of max HP.

## Action Point (AP) economy
All brothers and most enemies have 9 AP, fully refreshed at the start of each round (stuns/goblin poison/some injuries reduce recovery); a few units have less (Wiedergangers) or more (Direwolves, Goblin Wolfriders). Movement costs 2 AP on flat ground (grass/plains/roads), 3 AP on rough tiles (forest/snow), 4 AP on murky swamp water, plus +1 AP per tile when changing elevation (Pathfinder perk removes terrain/elevation AP penalties). Most 1-handed attacks cost 4 AP (dagger with Mastery: 3), 2-handed attacks 6 AP (exceptions: Warbrand, Rhomphaia, 2H cleavers 4 AP; Jagged Pike 5 AP). Battles start with lines at least 5 hexes apart, so humans cannot reach melee on round 1, but bows (range 7, 8 with mastery) and crossbows (range 6) are in firing range immediately. Berserk perk refunds 4 AP on a kill once per round; Quick Hands swaps items free once per round (except shield swaps).
WHY: 9 AP cleanly partitions into 'move a bit and attack once' or 'attack twice with a cheap weapon', making each turn a legible trade-off; weapon AP costs are a primary axis of weapon differentiation (two 4-AP swings vs one big 6-AP swing).

## Fatigue as a second action resource
Every skill use and movement also costs Fatigue against a max-Fatigue stat that is reduced by the weight of equipped armor, helmet, weapon and shield. A character at max Fatigue can neither move nor attack. 15 Fatigue is recovered at the start of each turn (Recover perk and Second Wind Potion restore more). Fatigue is also inflicted defensively: being struck adds +5 Fatigue (+10 from one-handed maces, +20 from two-handed maces; Fallen Heroes deal double); dodging or shield-blocking a melee attack adds +2 (blocking ranged attacks adds none). Orc weapons/shields add +5 Fatigue per skill use for humans. Zombies and Ancient Dead accumulate no fatigue at all; many elite enemies regenerate more than 15/turn.
WHY: Creates an attrition/pacing layer on top of AP: heavy armor and big weapons buy survivability and damage at the cost of stamina and turn order, and dedicated 'fatigue damage' weapons (maces) can lock enemies out of acting — a soft-control win condition.

## Initiative and turn order
Turn order each round is by current Initiative, highest first. A typical brother has ~100 Initiative, reduced 1:1 by currently accumulated Fatigue AND by any penalty to max Fatigue (armor/weapon weight). So a heavily armored or exhausted character slides toward the end of the order as the battle goes on. Fleeing units get −1000 initiative for turn order. The Dodge perk converts 15% of CURRENT Initiative into both Melee and Ranged Defense, so fatigue buildup directly erodes defense for light builds; Adrenaline guarantees early action next turn (ordered among Adrenaline users by initiative).
WHY: Couples three systems (gear weight, fatigue spending, turn order) into one number, making 'light and fast' vs 'heavy and slow' a real, continuously felt build choice rather than a flavor label.

## Two-layer health: head/body armor durability + hitpoints
Each character has three damage pools: Head Armor (helmet durability), Body Armor (armor durability), and Hitpoints. Each successful hit rolls armor damage and HP damage separately. Simplified formulas: armor damage = base damage × modifiers × weapon's % effectiveness vs armor; HP (bleed-through) damage = base damage × modifiers × weapon's % armor-ignoring direct damage − 0.1 × remaining armor on the struck part (cannot go negative; default armor mitigation multiplier is 0.1). If the hit destroys the armor (or armor is already 0), additional overflow HP damage = base × modifiers × (1 − armor-ignore %) − armor damage already dealt. Head hits multiply final HP damage ×1.5. Worked wiki example: Billhook (60–90 damage, 30% armor-ignore, 150% vs armor) vs Orc Warrior in 280 scale: rolls 120 armor damage and 27 direct, target takes 27 − 18 (10% of 180 remaining armor) = 9, ×1.5 head crit = 13 HP. Battle Forged reduces armor damage by armor×0.05% per point (30% at 300); Nimble lets light units take only 40% HP damage; Additional Fur Padding multiplies direct damage ×0.66.
WHY: Armor is an ablative second health bar per body part rather than a % reduction, creating a weapon rock-paper-scissors: high armor-effectiveness weapons (hammers/axes) shred armor, high armor-ignore weapons (crossbows, daggers' Puncture at 100% ignore/0% armor damage, barbarian 2H) bypass it, and the 10%-of-remaining-armor mitigation makes topped-up heavy armor feel meaningfully protective.

## Hit chance formula
hit chance = (attacker skill × skill multipliers) − (defender defense × defense multipliers), clamped to 5–95% for regular attacks (utility skills auto-hit at 100). A d100 is rolled; roll ≤ chance hits. Additive modifiers: skill-specific accuracy (Thrust +20, Slash/Impale/Hook/Prong/Repel +10, Overhead Strike +5, Round Swing −10/−15, Puncture −15, 2-tile attacks without mastery −15, Knock Back +25, ranged: Aimed Shot +10, Shoot Bolt +15, thrown +20), elevation ±10% per height level, surround +5% per extra adjacent attacker, shield defense bonuses. Multiplicative modifiers (applied to skill and/or defense): morale state (Confident ×1.1, Wavering ×0.9, Breaking ×0.8, Fleeing ×0.7), Nighttime ×0.7 on ranged skill and ranged defense, injuries (e.g. Broken Arm ×0.5 melee skill, Fractured Skull ×0.5), Drunk ×0.75, Swarm of Insects ×0.5, swamp terrain ×0.75 on melee skill and defense, Lone Wolf ×1.15. Defense has diminishing returns: every point above 50 counts half (defense = 50 + (defense−50)×0.5). Beginner difficulty silently shifts all rolls 5 points in the player's favor. Lucky trait rerolls one incoming hit.
WHY: A single transparent subtraction (skill − defense) the player can read in the tooltip, with the 5–95 clamp guaranteeing both hope and risk on every swing; the >50 defense soft cap curbs stacking while still rewarding it (each point past 50 still multiplies effective survivability more).

## Head hits as critical hits
After an attack hits, a second d100 determines body part: base 25% chance to hit the head. Modifiers: reach melee weapons +5, all two-handed swords +5, one-handed flails +10, two-handed flails +15, throwing axes +5, Head Hunter perk guarantees the next head hit after a body hit, flail skills Hail/Lash always hit the head (100%), Puncture and Terror can never hit the head (×0 multiplier). A head hit is a critical: ×1.5 HP damage (armor damage unaffected); 1H axes' Chop adds another +50% on head hits, Brute trait +15%; Steel Brow perk negates all bonus head damage. Damage is applied to head armor (helmet) instead of body armor, and the head-hit flag raises the injury bonus multiplier to 1.25.
WHY: Unifies crits with the body-part armor system: the answer to crits is literally buying a helmet, and flails/axes get a distinct identity as head-hunting weapons. Random helmet-or-armor targeting also makes both armor slots matter.

## Zone of Control (ZoC) and disengagement
Any melee-armed unit (or beast, except Alps/Hexen) exerts ZoC on its 6 adjacent tiles. A unit leaving a ZoC tile triggers a free attack (no fatigue cost) from EACH adjacent hostile; if all such attacks miss, the move succeeds; if any hits, the move is cancelled (AP/fatigue spent) and must be retried. Units with ranged weapons, fleeing, stunned, entangled, horrified or sleeping units exert no ZoC. Counters: Footwork skill (move through ZoC safely), Rotation (swap places with an ally), smoke pots (suppress ZoC on tiles for 1 turn). With ≥2 levels of height difference, adjacent units are not in melee range and exert no ZoC on each other.
WHY: Makes front lines 'sticky' and positioning consequential — engaging is cheap, disengaging is gambled HP — while the hit-cancels-movement rule (rather than just damage) makes pinning a genuinely reliable tactic and makes escape tools feel valuable.

## Surrounding / flanking bonus
Implemented as a melee-defense reduction on the surrounded unit: melee defense −= surround bonus × (adjacent enemies − 1), i.e. +5% effective hit chance per attacker beyond the first, default bonus 5 (Wiedergangers and Backstabber perk: 10; Underdog perk reduces it by 5, negating the standard bonus). The bonus applies to ALL attackers against the surrounded target, including non-adjacent polearm users, but stunned units and units holding ranged weapons do not count toward the surround, and ranged attacks don't benefit. Non-hostile third parties don't grant you surround. Because it is a defense debuff, it also applies to friendly-fire AoE swings against your own surrounded brothers (noted on the wiki as a likely dev oversight). Getting newly surrounded also triggers a negative morale check (modifier +50 − 0.1 × enemy XP value, capped at +10; being pushed/pulled into surround: +40).
WHY: Rewards encirclement and punishes overextension with a simple, stackable number; tying it to defense reduction (not attacker buff) elegantly makes it benefit everyone attacking the victim — but the friendly-fire interaction is a cautionary side effect of that implementation choice.

## Morale states and Resolve checks
Six states: Unbreakable (undead only, immune), Confident (×1.1 to resolve/melee & ranged skill/defenses), Steady (default, ×1), Wavering (×0.9), Breaking (×0.8), Fleeing (×0.7). Multipliers apply after all additive bonuses. Checks are hidden d100 rolls with success chance = Resolve + modifiers, capped at 95% success, floor 0%. Quantified triggers: taking ≥15 HP damage → negative check at −40 × missing-HP fraction; ally dies → +20 − 0.035 × ally XP value + distance^2.4 penalty; ally flees → +30 − 0.035 × XP + distance^3; enemy dies → positive check at −30 + 0.04 × enemy XP − distance^3 (killer gets +15); newly surrounded → +50 − 0.1 × enemy XP (≥ +10); adjacent allies give +3 each on negative checks, adjacent enemies −3 each; Fearsome perk imposes −0.15 × attacker's Resolve (cap −20). XP value of a brother = 100 + 30 × (level−1). Fleeing units cancel Shieldwall/Spearwall/Riposte/Indomitable, act last (−1000 initiative), can't be controlled, and rally on their own at +2 × rounds-fleeing when not adjacent to enemies; Rally the Troops grants checks at +0.4 × user's Resolve (−10 × distance if target not fleeing) and can't raise morale above Steady.
WHY: Morale is a parallel win condition (rout the enemy without killing everyone) and a cascade engine — one death triggers checks across both sides scaled by victim importance and proximity, producing emergent chain-routs and making Resolve/banner-bearer investment strategically meaningful.

## Temporary injuries (in-battle wound system)
A hit can inflict an injury if the target is injurable and HP damage ≥ 10. Whether a specific injury is possible: (injury threshold × multipliers) ≤ damage ÷ max hitpoints, where thresholds range 25–60% of max HP per injury; Crippling Strikes perk multiplies thresholds ×0.66, Iron Jaw trait ×1.25 (for the receiver), head hits set a ×1.25 bonus multiplier. One injury is picked uniformly at random among qualifying candidates of the weapon's damage type (blunt/cutting/piercing/burning, each with separate body and head injury lists). Injuries apply stat multipliers, e.g. Broken Arm ×0.5 melee skill, Fractured Skull ×0.5 skill and defense, Cut Leg Muscles ×0.6 melee defense; hemorrhaging head injuries (Grazed Neck 1 dmg/turn, Cut Artery 3/turn) bleed until bandaged. Injuries persist days after battle (temple treatment halves healing time, price scales with healing days and character level: 20 × settlement multiplier × days × (1 + (level−1)×0.2)), drop mood, and can fester. Some enemies are immune (Alps, Schrats, zombies, Ancient Dead...); Orc Berserkers receive injuries but ignore their effects.
WHY: Big hits do more than HP damage — they degrade fighting capacity mid-battle and impose multi-day campaign costs, linking the tactical and strategic layers; scaling thresholds by % of max HP means tanky characters shrug off wounds smaller fighters would suffer.

## Permanent injuries and death-saving ('struck down')
When a brother's HP reaches 0 he has a 33% chance (90% with the Survivor trait) to be 'struck down' instead of killed, surviving with one random permanent injury (Brain Damage, Missing Eye, Broken Knee, Partly Collapsed Lung, Traumatized, etc. — the body part hit does not influence which). Survival is impossible if the killing blow was a fatality, the character was retreating, or the damage was self-inflicted/DoT (bleed, poison). Permanent injuries are incurable except by the unique Water Skin legendary item; examples of effects appear in hit-chance tables (Missing Eye ×0.5 ranged skill, Broken Knee ×0.6 defense, Broken Elbow Joint ×0.7/×0.8). Struck-down brothers can't be eaten by Nachzehrers or reanimated as undead. Enemies at 0 HP always die (barring Nine Lives perk / Wiederganger resurrection).
WHY: Softens permadeath into a dramatic middle outcome: veterans usually survive a downing but carry visible scars forever, generating attachment, war stories, and hard retire-or-keep decisions without removing the threat of true loss.

## Shields and Shieldwall
Shields add flat Melee/Ranged Defense and a max-Fatigue penalty: Buckler +10/+5 (−4 fatigue, no Shieldwall), Wooden Shield +15/+15 (−10), Adarga +15/+20, Kite Shield +15/+25 (−16, ranged-defense oriented), Sipar +18/+18, Heater Shield +20/+15 (−14, melee oriented), Ancient Tower Shield +20/+20. The Shieldwall skill (4 AP, 20 fatigue class of cost) doubles the shield's defense bonus until next turn, +5 more per adjacent ally also in Shieldwall; Shield Expert perk adds +25% to shield bonuses (and applies inside Shieldwall). Shield durability: −1 per melee hit blocked, more from arrows/bolts/thrown weapons; axes' Split Shield skill deals heavy shield damage (+50% with Axe Mastery); Shield Expert halves shield damage taken. Some attacks ignore shield defense entirely: flail skills (Flail, Pound, and Hail/Lash with mastery) and Cascade — though Shieldwall's bonus still applies. Knock Back (shield skill): 4 AP, 20 fatigue, +25 to hit, no damage, pushes 1 tile.
WHY: Shields are the main purchasable defense and define the front-line tank role (with the fatigue tax keeping them honest); shield-ignoring flails and shield-destroying axes exist specifically as counters, producing equipment-level counterplay.

## Ranged combat: distance falloff, cover, scatter
Ranged hit chance = Ranged Skill − Ranged Defense plus skill accuracy and per-tile falloff (first tile free for bows/crossbows, counted for thrown): bow Quick Shot −4%/hex (no bonus), bow Aimed Shot −2%/hex (+10 base), crossbow Shoot Bolt −3%/hex (+15 base, +10 for Spiked Impaler), thrown weapons −10%/hex (+20–30 base, making them deadly close and useless far). Cover ('line of fire blocked') multiplies hit chance ×0.25 (×0.5 with Bullseye); a failed blocked shot 'goes astray' to a random blocking obstacle/unit at an extra −15%. Shots over allies at exactly 2 tiles are not blocked. Scatter: a missed projectile at distance >2 can hit a target adjacent to the aim point if the target has no shield, or the skill ignores shields, or the roll exceeded chance + (shield+shieldwall bonus)×2 (AI shooters: flat 25% chance, requires no adjacent ally); scattered shots can land 1 hex beyond max weapon range and deal ×0.75 damage when astray. Each height level grants +1 range and improved hit chance downhill. Vision (default 7) limits targeting; heavy helmets reduce it.
WHY: Per-skill falloff curves and cover create a real positioning game for archers (height, lanes of fire) instead of flat % to hit, while scatter makes firing into melee scrums tempting but genuinely dangerous to friends — a constant, flavorful risk decision.

## Terrain and high ground
The battlefield has 4 elevation tiers (0–3). Attacking from higher ground gives +10% hit chance; attacking uphill costs −10% per level of difference. Each height level gives ranged units +1 hex range and better vision. Climbing costs +1 AP per tile plus extra fatigue (Pathfinder negates the AP cost). Adjacent units with ≥2 levels of height difference are not in melee range and don't exert ZoC on each other. Terrain tiles change move cost (2/3/4 AP) and swamp multiplies melee skill and defense ×0.75. The AI actively seeks high ground, especially for ranged units.
WHY: Elevation converts the map itself into a contested resource — a hill is effectively +20% swing in every exchange across it plus archer range — making the pre-battle deployment and early-round race for terrain tactically loaded.

## Night battles and the day/night cycle
The world runs Dawn→Morning→Midday→Afternoon→Evening→Dusk→Night. Battles at night apply the Nighttime status to all non-immune combatants: −2 Vision, −30% Ranged Skill (×0.7) and −30% Ranged Defense (×0.7). Melee stats are untouched. Since attack skill values generally exceed defense values, the net effect favors melee and the defender; the wiki explicitly recommends attacking ranged-heavy enemy groups at night. Night Owl Elixir and The Fangshire helmet grant immunity.
WHY: A strategic-layer timing decision (when to force a fight) that mechanically rebalances the tactical layer — a cheap way to let players counter enemy compositions (e.g., goblin archers) through scheduling rather than stats.

## Weapon class differentiation
Classes are differentiated by AP cost, fatigue cost, % effectiveness vs armor, % armor-ignoring direct damage, special skills, and head-hit modifiers, not just damage ranges. Examples from the wiki: swords get accuracy skills (Slash +10, Riposte); spears Thrust +20 accuracy and Spearwall (free attacks at ×0.5 damage on approachers); daggers' Puncture has 0% armor damage but 100% direct-to-HP damage at −15 accuracy and can't headshot; hammers have armor-destruction skills (Destroy/Demolish Armor ×1.5–2 armor damage, 0 HP damage, min 10); 1H/2H maces add +10/+20 fatigue damage on hit and stun (Knock Out ×0.5 damage, +2×5 fatigue); cleavers/whips cause bleeding DoT (5 dmg over 2 turns, 10 with Cleaver Mastery, needs ≥6 HP damage to proc); axes get Split Shield and Chop (+50% HP damage on head hits); flails ignore shield defense and have +10/+15 head-hit chance with guaranteed-head skills (Hail/Lash); polearms attack at 2-tile range (reach weapons +5 head chance, 2-tile attacks −15 accuracy without mastery); 2H weapons get AoE arcs (Round Swing −10/−15 accuracy, Split Man hits head on crit with a second half-damage strike). Double-gripping a 1H weapon with empty off-hand gives +25% damage (+25% direct with Duelist). Orc weapons cost humans +5 fatigue per use.
WHY: Every weapon family answers a different defensive layer (shields, armor, HP, fatigue, morale), so loadout choice is matchup-driven; reimplementers should differentiate weapons along resource axes (AP/fatigue/armor interaction/special effects) rather than DPS alone.

## Weapon and gear durability
Melee weapons lose 3 durability when striking armor that has ≥50 durability remaining; they never degrade hitting bare flesh. AoE attacks pay durability per connecting hit (a Warscythe can lose 9 on a 3-hit Reap). Bows and crossbows lose 2 durability per shot. Shields lose 1 durability per ordinary blocked melee hit, more from projectiles, and large amounts from Split Shield (weapon-dependent, +50% with Axe Mastery); Shield Expert halves shield damage taken (min 1). Broken items are destroyed/unusable; repairs cost tools on the world map.
WHY: A slow economic drain that makes prolonged fights and shield-heavy tactics cost campaign resources, and makes shield-breaking a viable mid-fight strategy against turtling enemies.

## Secondary damage and special damage rules
DoT and special damage bypass armor: Bleeding (5/turn for 2 turns, 10 with Cleaver Mastery; requires ≥6 HP damage on the proc hit; undead immune; cured by bandages), Poison (Webknecht: 5/turn for 3 turns, needs ≥7 HP damage), Acid (strips 10–20% of current armor durability per turn for 3 turns), falling damage (10 per height level when knocked off a ledge), Alp Nightmare (max(5, 25 − 0.25 × Resolve)). Resilient perk cuts DoT duration to 1 turn; Indomitable halves all incoming damage; Nimble does NOT protect against secondary damage. Fatigue-damaging skills deal 5 × multiplier fatigue (Bash ×3 total, Cudgel/Strike Down ×5).
WHY: Armor-bypassing channels keep heavy-armor builds from being unconditionally safe and give 'light' enemy types (spiders, hounds, cleaver users) a way to threaten tanks; Resolve doubling as anti-magic defense adds value to a social stat.

- Use two stacked per-turn resources with different time horizons: AP resets every round (tactical spending) while Fatigue accumulates and recovers slowly (strategic pacing). Linking accumulated fatigue to initiative and to Dodge-style defenses makes exhaustion felt everywhere without extra UI.
- Model armor as ablative pools per body part (helmet vs body) rather than percentage damage reduction, and give every weapon two knobs — % effectiveness vs armor and % damage that bypasses armor — plus a small mitigation term (HP damage reduced by 10% of remaining armor). This single design yields weapon identity, gear progression, and counterplay (armor-shredders vs armor-piercers) simultaneously.
- Keep the hit formula legible (attacker skill − defender defense, additive situational modifiers, 5–95% clamp) and apply soft caps as diminishing returns (defense points above 50 count half) instead of hard caps — players can still chase the stat, but degenerate stacking is curbed.
- Make crits spatial: a second roll for head vs body (base 25%) with +50% damage on head hits routed to the helmet armor pool. This makes crit defense purchasable equipment (a helmet) and lets weapons differentiate via head-hit chance (flails) — far more interesting than a flat crit stat.
- Quantify morale: a 6-state ladder with flat ×1.1/×0.9/×0.8/×0.7 stat multipliers per state, and event-driven checks whose modifiers scale with victim importance (XP value) and distance (distance^2.4 / distance^3 falloff). This produces emergent chain-routs and a non-lethal victory condition with very little code.
- Make Zone-of-Control departures fail on a hit, not merely deal damage — pinning becomes reliable, and escape skills (Footwork, Rotation, smoke) become meaningful purchases.
- Implement surround/flanking as a defense debuff on the victim so all attackers benefit automatically — but beware the side effect the wiki flags as a dev oversight: the debuff also boosts friendly-fire AoE hits against your own surrounded units. Decide explicitly whether flanking modifiers apply to friendly fire.
- Couple tactics to the campaign through injuries: gate them on damage ≥10 HP and on damage as a fraction of max HP (thresholds 25–60%), and convert lethal blows into a 33% 'struck down with a permanent injury' save. Scars create attachment and consequence while softening permadeath frustration.
- Use global condition modifiers (night: ×0.7 ranged skill and ranged defense; swamp: ×0.75 melee stats; high ground: ±10%/level) to let players counter enemy compositions via when/where they fight, not only with builds.
- Hidden rolls breed confusion: the wiki documents player misunderstanding because the cover/obstruction roll and Knock Back rolls are not shown in the combat log, and morale checks are entirely invisible. Log every roll or expect wikis and forums to reverse-engineer (and mistrust) your math.
- Keep one source of truth for data: the wiki notes weapon-file 'direct damage' values are used only for UI while actual values live in skill definitions, causing tooltips to lie (e.g., the Goedendag). Several pages also carry 'Outdated since DLC X' banners — data-driven design with generated tooltips/docs avoids both problems.
- Cheap difficulty knob that preserves all content: Beginner mode just shifts every hit roll 5 points in the player's favor and delays crises, rather than changing enemy stats or gear — the simulation stays authentic across difficulties.
- Watch interaction edge cases between status systems: immunity interactions (e.g., a pike's Repel that knocks back AND staggers silently does neither against stagger-immune enemies) read as bugs to players; define how multi-effect skills resolve against partial immunities.
- Differentiate weapon classes along resource axes — AP per swing (4 vs 6 of a 9-AP pool), fatigue cost, armor interaction, reach (2-tile polearms with −15 accuracy unless mastered), shield-ignoring, DoT, fatigue damage, head-hunting — so 'best weapon' is matchup-dependent rather than a DPS ranking.

## Battle Brothers — campaign/strategic layer (wiki digest)

SUMMARY: The Battle Brothers wiki documents a campaign layer built on a procedurally generated world map (~17 settlements split among 3 noble houses) where a mercenary company loops between contracts, trade, looting, and recruitment, with three meta-progression currencies: XP/levels for individual men, gold for upkeep and gear, and Renown for contract pay and access. Precise rules are documented for nearly everything: background-based recruit stat ranges with hidden talent stars (60/30/10% for 1/2/3 stars) that widen level-up rolls, an XP curve to level 11 plus diminished "veteran levels," a 7-tier perk tree gated by points spent, formula-driven contract pay/difficulty scaling (renown^0.39-style multipliers, roster-strength scaling), settlement price modifiers driven by settlement size/attached locations/relations/situations, four late-game crises that begin around day 80-100, ambition-driven mini-goals worth +100 renown each, and a retirement system with 5 endings and a numeric score.

## Character backgrounds (recruit generation)
Every recruit has a background (Farmhand, Thief, Sellsword, Hedge Knight, Swordmaster, Beggar, etc.) that defines: starting attribute ranges (a shared base range plus per-background modifiers; Apprentice is the unmodified baseline: HP 50-60, Fatigue 90-100, Resolve 30-40, Initiative 100-110, Melee Skill 47-57, Ranged Skill 32-42, Melee/Ranged Defense 0-5), starting equipment, base hiring cost (30-150 gold, Swordmaster 400), base daily wage (3-35), allowed/excluded traits and talents, background-specific events and dialog, XP-rate modifiers (Apprentice +10%, Mason +5%, Wildman -15%), and the character's epilogue fate at retirement. Examples: Hedge Knight HP 62-73, MSk 58-67, MDef 6-10, starts level 2-5, comes with heavy gear; Swordmaster MSk 72-77, MDef 10-20 but low HP 38-48 and eventually gains the Old trait; Beggar HP 40-50, Resolve 20-35. Military backgrounds start at level 1-5 with unspent level-ups the player distributes after hire. Recruits are found in settlement 'Recruits' pools (attached locations bias the pool: barracks attract Militia/Deserters/Sellswords/Hedge Knights, lumber camps attract Lumberjacks) or gained free via events.
WHY: Backgrounds give every hire a story and a price/power tradeoff, creating a recruitment economy where cheap high-ceiling backgrounds (Brawler, Wildman, Thief) compete with expensive reliable ones (Hedge Knight, Sellsword). Tying events and endings to backgrounds makes the roster feel like characters, not stat blocks.

## Talent stars (hidden potential)
On hire, each brother gets stars on exactly 3 of his 8 attributes, assigned randomly (per star count: 60% chance 1 star, 30% 2 stars, 10% 3 stars), independent of background except a few exclusions (e.g., Hedge Knight can't roll Initiative/Ranged Skill talents, Brawler/Butcher/Swordmaster can't roll Ranged Skill). Stars widen level-up rolls: e.g., Melee Skill rolls 1-3 points/level with no stars, 2-3 with 1 star, exactly 3 with 2 stars, 3-4 with 3 stars (Initiative is one higher across the board; HP/Fatigue/Resolve/Ranged Skill/Ranged Defense roll 2-4 base up to 4-5 at 3 stars). Rule of thumb documented on the wiki: each star is worth ~+5 points in that stat by level 11, so a 50-Ranged-Skill recruit with one star equals a 55-skill recruit with none.
WHY: Stars create slot-machine excitement at the recruiter and long-term growth differentiation without changing immediate power; the '1 star = 5 starting stat points' equivalence makes evaluating recruits a satisfying optimization puzzle.

## Traits and try-out
Recruits carry 0-2 hidden traits (Iron Lungs, Strong, Tough, Greedy, Fragile, Dumb, Bright, etc.), constrained by background (premium backgrounds exclude most bad traits). Before hiring, a 'try-out' fee — min(hiring cost − 25, 25 + 10% of hiring cost) — reveals traits but not talents or stat rolls. Titles in a recruit's name can hint at one trait.
WHY: Paid partial information turns hiring into a push-your-luck decision; keeping talents hidden even after try-out preserves uncertainty so money can't fully de-risk recruitment.

## Leveling and XP
Levels 1-11 require cumulative XP: 200/500/1000/2000/3500/5000/7000/9000/12000/15000. Each level grants 1 perk point and lets the player raise 3 of 8 attributes by the talent-modified roll. Above 11, 'veteran levels' need 4000+1000×(level−11) XP, award NO perk point, and cap rolls at +1 to three stats; hard cap level 33. Combat XP: the killer gets 20% of a kill's XP, the other 80% splits equally across the party; global −15% XP modifier always applies; modifiers are multiplicative (Beginner difficulty +10%, Training Hall buffs +50%/+35%/+20% for 1/3/5 battles, Student perk +20% until level 11, Bright +10%, Dumb −15%, tiny squads −15% at 2 men / −30% at 1).
WHY: A short, steep power curve (11 meaningful levels) keeps every level-up a major event, while veteran levels offer asymptotic, perk-free growth so endgame characters plateau instead of scaling forever.

## Perk tree
7 tiers of perks (Tier 1: Fast Adaptation, Colossus, Student, Pathfinder...; Tier 4: weapon Masteries; Tier 6: Nimble vs Battle Forged; Tier 7: Fearsome, Duelist, Killing Frenzy, Indomitable). 1 perk point per level (10 lifetime for a normal bro reaching 11). Tier N+1 unlocks after spending N points anywhere in unlocked rows — i.e., you must invest in lower tiers to reach higher ones, but choices within rows are free. Perk resets only via a consumable (Potion of Oblivion); an extra point only from a rare item (Mysterious Jug).
WHY: Spending-gated tiers force meaningful early commitments and prevent rushing capstones; scarce respecs make builds consequential and motivate hiring new recruits rather than rebuilding old ones.

## Procedural world map
The map is randomly generated per campaign: typically 17 settlements (2 City Halls, 3 Town Halls, 6 Village Halls, 2 Strongholds, 2 Castles, 2 Fortified Manors) all assigned to one of 3 procedurally generated noble houses (split can be 6/6/5 or as lopsided as 10/5/2). Terrain types (grassland, forest, swamp, hills/mountains, snow, desert) modify overland speed, sight radius, food consumption, ambient events (mountain injury events), and seed the tactical battlefield's tile costs (e.g., swamp battles get Murky Water tiles at 4AP/14 Fatigue plus a 25% combat-skill penalty; forest tiles 3AP/6 Fatigue). Enemy camps are revealed by contracts and exploration and can be destroyed for loot/renown. Real-time-with-pause overland clock: a full day ≈ 1m45s, with a dawn-to-night cycle; vendors close at night, travel slows, sight shrinks, wages paid daily at noon.
WHY: Procedural geography makes each campaign's economy and contract logistics unique (trade depends on what attached locations spawned where), while terrain links the strategic and tactical layers so where you pick a fight matters.

## Settlements, buildings and attached locations
Civilian settlements (Village/Town/City) trade, recruit, and offer contracts; fortified ones (Fortified Manor/Castle/Stronghold/Citadel) sell military-grade gear, host noble contracts (unlocked at Professional renown, 1050), and spawn patrols. Buildings: Marketplace (stock refreshes every 3 days), Weaponsmith/Armorer (prices ~25% above market, full-durability items, instant paid repair), Fletcher, Temple (pays to halve injury heal times, e.g., 2-4 days → 1-2), Training Hall (paid XP buffs, price scales with trainee level: Veteran's Lessons 253g at L1 vs 1106g at L10), Tavern (rumors, morale drinks, open 24h), Kennel, Barber, Taxidermist (trophy crafting), Arena (3-man fights; every 5th match is special and awards unique gladiator gear), Harbor (instant sea travel priced by distance × party size), Alchemist. Each settlement has 1-8 'attached locations' (wheat fields, iron veins, lumber camps, dye makers, herbalist groves...) that determine which goods/supplies are produced (cheap locally) and which recruits appear; destroyed locations stop producing until rebuilt.
WHY: Attached locations make settlements legible economic nodes — players learn to route via the herbalist village for cheap medicine and sell furs in the distant city — turning the map into a logistics puzzle rather than interchangeable rest stops.

## Market price model
Every settlement has linked buy/sell price modifiers. Regular items sell at ~13-18% of worth (expert difficulty), treasures ~6.33× that (≈95% of worth), trade goods ~7.48× (can exceed 100%). Base multiplier by settlement size: small 95%, medium 102%, large 110%, plus 3% per attached location (3-8 locations). Relations adjust additively: below 50, ±(50−relations)×0.6% (penalty doubled); above 50, ±(relations−50)×0.3% (max ±15%) — better relations raise sell prices and cut buy prices. Settlement situations multiply on top (Ambushed Trade Routes +10% sell, Safe Roads +9%, Well Supplied −10% buy). Trade goods produced locally sell to you at normal buy price but only fetch 15% if sold back there; non-local goods cost 150% to buy and sell at 110%. Supplies in some settlements cost 1.5× expected. Economic difficulty cuts sell prices ~10% per step.
WHY: A fully deterministic, multi-factor price model rewards system mastery: the buy-low-in-villages/sell-high-in-cities loop emerges from rules instead of scripted arbitrage, and relations become an economic investment.

## Trade goods
12 trade goods with fixed base worth: Peat Bricks 100, Cloth Rolls 140, Quality Wood 180 (also a crafting ingredient for the Masterwork Bow), Copper Ingots 220, Amber Shards 260, Furs 300, Spices 320, Salt 340, Incense 380, Dyes 400, Silk 460, Uncut Gems 520. Produced only by specific attached locations (Salt Mine, Silk Farm, Gem Mine...), some exclusive to the southern desert region; also lootable from caravans/camps. They sell at full worth (vs 15% for ordinary loot), subject to the local/non-local and price-modifier rules above.
WHY: High-density, full-value cargo gives a pacifist income stream and a reason to traverse the map; regional exclusivity (southern goods) rewards long-haul risk.

## Supplies and upkeep (food/tools/medicine/ammo)
Each brother eats 2 provisions/day (more on harsh terrain; modified by Gluttonous/Spartan traits); starvation tanks mood and eventually causes desertion. ~17 food types each with worth (50-150 per 25 units) and spoil times (7-16 days, expensive food lasts longer); brothers auto-eat nearest-to-spoiling first; food variety triggers mood events. Tools: 1 tool repairs 15 durability; gear repairs automatically at 3 durability/hour while traveling, doubled while camped; carrying caps 150 (200 on Beginner). Medicine: 1 unit per injury per day to heal temporary injuries; cap 100-150. Ammo: 100 crowns per 50 ammo, refills quivers after battle. Retinue followers modify all this (Quartermaster +50 caps, Blacksmith +33% repair speed and prevents item destruction, Cook +33% HP regen and +3 days food spoil, Scavenger recovers tools from destroyed enemy armor). HP regenerates at 1/hour marching, 2/hour camping; wages paid daily at noon, missing pay drops mood toward desertion.
WHY: Four consumable resources create constant burn-rate pressure so victory in battle still costs money (repairs, healing, ammo), making 'won the fight, lost the campaign' attrition the core strategic tension.

## Hiring cost and wage formulas
Hiring cost = background base (30-150; Swordmaster 400) + 1.25× value of carried equipment + 500×(level−1)^1.5 for pre-leveled recruits (0 to ~3000). Daily wage = background base (3-35) × random 90-110% × 1.1^(level−1) for levels 1-11 (up to 2.6×), then ×1.03^(level−11) for veteran levels (e.g., Hedge Knight: 91/day at L11, 122/day at L21). Wages also +2 per level-up and +2 for the Greedy trait. Lone Wolf origin's player character draws no wage.
WHY: Exponential per-level wages make a roster of 20 veterans financially punishing, forcing companies to balance elite size against payroll — an elegant soft cap on snowballing.

## Contracts (missions)
One active contract at a time, taken in settlements (gold-coin marker shows targets on map). ~18 civilian types (Drive Away Bandits, Escort Caravan, beast hunts, Investigate Cemetery...), ~21 noble types (Patrol, Decisive Battle, Destroy Orc Camp, Raid Caravan...; require Professional renown 1050 via the Make Nobles Aware ambition), ~10 southern/city-state types. Pay = type base value (400 for Return Item up to 2000 for Find Artifact) × random 90-110% × skull-difficulty multiplier (easy 63-81%, medium 94-107%, hard 120-148%, raised to power 1.3) × renown multiplier ((Renown×0.003)^0.39, clamped 1.35-2.7) × economic difficulty. Completion: +25 renown, +10 relations (civilian) or +5 (noble); poor completion +5/+2.5 relations but −25 renown; failing −75 renown/−20 or −10 relations; canceling −100 renown; betrayal −200 renown/−100 relations. Difficulty (enemy spawns) scales with type base (40-200), skull multiplier (70-135%), roster strength = Σ(10+2×(level−1)) over your 12 strongest men, via 0.94×(strength/100)^0.89 clamped 0.75-5.0, × combat difficulty (Beginner 85%, Veteran 100%, Expert 115%). Renown affects pay but NOT difficulty. Non-contract camps/roaming parties instead scale with distance-from-civilization and elapsed time, barely with player strength.
WHY: Splitting scaling — contracts track player power, world locations track time and distance — guarantees fair paid work while preserving a dangerous open world that can outpace or fall behind the player; renown-gated noble contracts give a midgame goal.

## Contract haggling (Annoyance system)
Negotiation options: more total pay (+4-11%), more advance (+25% shifted from completion), more completion pay, more per-head pay (25% shifted, often lossy via rounding). Each haggle adds hidden Annoyance, a random float 3-6 (halved with the Negotiator follower), accumulated before the roll; failure chance = Annoyance×10%, so first attempt fails 30-60% (avg 45%), and reaching 9+ Annoyance ejects you with a big reputation hit. Each attempt also costs 0.5 relations (waived with Negotiator). Only 'more total pay' is net-positive; advance/per-head options just reallocate and typically lose money to rounding.
WHY: A hidden accumulating risk meter turns a menu interaction into push-your-luck gambling; one safe haggle, risky second, near-impossible third is an easily-learned rhythm.

## Renown / Reputation / Relations (three standing tracks)
Renown (company fame): named tiers from Treacherous (−500) through Unknown (0), Recognized (250), Professional (1050), Legendary (4000) to Immortal (14000); changes: contract complete +25, ambition +100, battle won +5, camp destroyed +10, daily decay −3 (guide says −2), retreat −15, poor completion −25, fail −75, cancel −100, betrayal −200; drives contract pay and unlocks. Relations (per-settlement, 0-100, start 50/Neutral; tiers Hostile 0-9 ... Allied 90-100): civilian contract +10, noble +5, favor +15, tavern round +0.1; attacking them −30, failing civilian contract −20, killing one unit −0.5; drifts 0.25/day back toward 50 (a completed contract's ~+8 decays in ~33 days); fortified settlements share their noble house's relation. Reputation (moral, 0-100): tracked but nearly vestigial — at 80+ you stop receiving Raze Attached Location contracts, plus a couple of origin events.
WHY: Separating professional fame (pay), local friendship (prices), and morality (content gating) lets players be famous butchers or anonymous saints; decay on renown and relations keeps standing a maintained resource rather than a ratchet. Reputation is also a documented cautionary tale: a tracked stat with almost no payoff.

## Settlement situations (dynamic local states)
Settlements can hold multiple simultaneous temporary states (~45 documented: Ambushed Trade Routes, Well Supplied, Raided, Besieged, Conquered, Drought, Witch Burnings, Tournament...). Each multiplies the settlement's price modifiers (after relations), changes item/recruit availability (including named-item chances), and signals which contracts will be offered there. Some are caused by world simulation (caravans intercepted, raids) and can be cleared by player contracts; with permanent destruction off, crises apply situations instead of deleting settlements.
WHY: Situations make the world readable and reactive — a 'Besieged' tag is simultaneously a price opportunity, a quest hook, and feedback that the simulation is running without the player.

## Noble houses and world simulation
Three procedurally generated noble houses with unique coats of arms, names and leaders own all settlements. Castles/strongholds spawn patrols that police roads and defenders when threats approach; watchtowers extend this. Settlements have a prosperity rating: every safely arrived trading caravan raises it; higher prosperity = more militia defenders (possibly with a Militia Captain morale anchor) and better market stock. Two caravan types (weakly-guarded trading caravans between any settlements; well-guarded noble supply caravans within a house) can be attacked by the player only when relations with the source are Threatening or lower (Ctrl+Click, name turns red). Brigands, greenskins, undead and beasts roam, fight each other, ambush caravans, and raid villages independent of the player.
WHY: A lightweight living-world sim (caravans → prosperity → militia/stock) makes player actions ripple through the economy and gives the late-game crises something real to destroy.

## Late game crises
Four crises: War of Noble Houses (houses capture rival settlements, reducing supply/wealth but not destroying them; siding with one house makes the other hostile and severs sea routes; special contracts: pitched battles, raiding caravans/settlements, sieges, relieving sieges), Undead Scourge (new undead locations spawn, raid-party generation increases, dead humans can rise as wiedergangers, settlements can be overrun/destroyed; contracts: destroy infested locations, reclaim an artifact, break sieges), Greenskin Invasion (orc/goblin camps spawn and besiege cities — ruins around a city signal a nearby greenskin city to destroy; contracts: rescue prisoners, relieve sieges, kill Orc Warlords), Holy War (north vs southern city states fight over 3 holy sites; player picks a side via contracts). The first crisis can be chosen at campaign start or randomized; it is foretold around day 50-70 and starts day 80-100 (Beginner combat difficulty and Ironman each delay it 5 days); a minimum elapsed-days plus minimum roster (average level and headcount) is required to trigger. A crisis runs 30-100+ days; the next can arrive within 100 days after at least 50 days of peace; the same crisis never repeats back-to-back. An optional 'permanent destruction' setting lets invasions delete settlements forever; otherwise they get negative situations. Resolving crises is done by killing raiding parties and destroying spawned bases; a 'defeat crisis' ambition shows progress but isn't required.
WHY: Crises convert the sandbox into a timed test of the company you built, raise stakes via optional permadeath for the map itself, and create distinct strategic textures (political war that locks map access vs. extermination wars against the map's destruction).

## Ambitions
Periodically the player picks one ambition from an offered set (≈45 total: Roster of 12/16/20, Have 10000/50000 Crowns, Player Banner, Make Nobles Aware, Win Arena Fights, Defeat <crisis>, Trade Goods = buy and sell 25 goods, etc.). All award +100 renown and a company-wide mood boost; some add items (Player Banner → Battle Standard for 1000 crowns, Sergeant Sash) or unlocks (Make Nobles Aware requires 800 renown and 2 completed ambitions and Player Banner; completing it at 1050 renown unlocks noble contracts and a map-model upgrade). Canceling costs company mood and 1-2 days' wait; 'No Ambition' (available after Make Nobles Aware) waits 3 days. Critical quirk: eligibility is checked only when the selection screen opens; many ambitions get permanently auto-marked completed with no reward if their condition is already met at that moment (documented list of ~15 affected, e.g., Roster of 12 if you already have 12 men), though auto-completion still unlocks dependent ambitions.
WHY: Ambitions are player-chosen short-term goals that pace the sandbox, feed the renown economy, and gate progression milestones — but the snapshot-eligibility design is a known pain point that punishes players for achieving goals before declaring them.

## Events (narrative pop-ups) and Mood
Events fire on the world map, filtered by requirements: specific backgrounds present (Aging Swordmaster, Brawler vs Brawler...), traits, active crises, current contract, origin, terrain (e.g., mountain injury events), reputation, food variety, etc. Outcomes grant/remove items, stats (+1 Fatigue from climbing), recruits, mood changes, or chains (Cultist Finale). Mood is a per-brother 0-100% scale in 7 named states (Angry 0-15 ... Euphoric 86-100) that trends daily toward 50 (Content) and converts into starting combat morale: Euphoric = 75% chance to start Confident, Eager 50%, Good Spirits 25%; Dissatisfied caps at Steady, Disgruntled at Wavering, Angry at Breaking and risks desertion. Mood drops from unpaid wages, hunger, fallen brothers, uncompensated dismissals; rises from victories and completed ambitions.
WHY: Events monetize the background system into emergent storytelling, and mood is the glue tying strategic management failures (no food, no pay) to tactical consequences (worse starting morale), making logistics emotionally legible.

## Retirement, endings and score
Optional campaign closure with 5 endings: Lessons Learned (game lost/no brothers), Early Retirement (retire before completing Make Nobles Aware), Bitter End (renown ≥1100 + Make Nobles Aware done; two epilogue variants), Leaving a Mark (1 crisis ended; unique epilogue per crisis), Leaving a Legacy (renown ≥6000 + 2 crises ended). In the first three the company dissolves and survivors fare poorly; in the last two the company outlives the captain and brothers get good epilogues (flavored by their backgrounds). Score = [Σ(item value×0.002) + 25 per named item + 100 per legendary item + (renown−100)×0.25] × 1.25^(crises ended) ÷ days elapsed, ×10.
WHY: Graded endings give the open-ended sandbox a victory condition ladder, and dividing score by elapsed days rewards efficiency over grinding — an explicit anti-snowball scoring choice.

## Difficulty settings and Ironman
Separate Combat and Economic difficulty plus starting funds tier. Combat: harder settings spawn harder enemy groups sooner; Beginner gives ±5 to-hit swing in player's favor and +10% XP; Beginner and Ironman each delay the first crisis 5 days. Economic: each step reduces contract pay 10%; Expert reduces sell prices 10% and deserters steal their gear; Beginner raises resource caps (+200 ammo, +50 tools, +50 medicine). Starting funds High/Medium/Low: 2500/2000/1500 crowns, 50 food, 40/20/10 tools, 80/40/20 ammo, 30/20/10 medicine. Enemy equipment stats and shop availability never change with difficulty. Ironman: autosave-only, no manual saves.
WHY: Decoupling combat pressure from economic pressure lets players tune which kind of struggle they want; keeping enemy stats constant preserves learnable combat math at all difficulties.

## Company origins
Campaign-start templates (Warriors of the North DLC) altering starting roster, gear, resources, renown (−100 to 200), and persistent rules: Lone Wolf (single wage-free hero), Trading Caravan (buy at 90%/sell at 110%, trade-focused), Peasant Militia (14-man battle line but restricted backgrounds), Manhunters (Indebted earn +10% XP, overseers −10%), Northern Raiders (unique guaranteed-3-star-Resolve Monk), Beast Slayers (trades at 110%/90%), Deserters, Cultists, Anatomists, Gladiators, Oathtakers (reputation-driven events), etc.
WHY: Origins are replayability multipliers that re-weight the same campaign systems (economy, roster size, XP flow) rather than adding new content, a cheap way to create distinct runs.

- Separate scaling regimes by content type: Battle Brothers scales contract opposition to roster strength (sum of 10+2×(level−1) over best 12 men) but scales open-world camps/parties by distance-from-civilization and elapsed days. This keeps paid work fair while leaving a genuinely dangerous world — copy this split rather than globally level-scaling everything.
- Beware perverse incentives in power-based scaling: because every extra man adds roster strength, the wiki openly recommends keeping a small roster to face easier contracts. If you scale to party size/level, ensure marginal recruits add more power than they add difficulty.
- Hidden-but-learnable randomness sells recruitment: talent stars (60/30/10% rarity) plus paid trait reveal (try-out ≈10% of hire cost) makes hiring a layered gamble where money buys partial information. The community's '1 star ≈ 5 stat points by max level' equivalence shows players love deriving such exchange rates — design systems with clean implicit math.
- Cap meaningful progression sharply: 11 levels with perk points, then perk-less veteran levels with +1 rolls and ballooning XP costs. This prevents infinite verticality and shifts late-game investment to breadth (new recruits, gear, crises) instead of grinding one super-unit.
- Make upkeep exponential, not linear: daily wage ×1.1 per level means a veteran company bleeds gold, naturally pruning roster bloat without hard caps. Pair income (renown-multiplied contract pay, clamped 1.35-2.7×) against this burn to create a treadmill that never fully resolves.
- Drive economy from legible structural factors: price = settlement size + 3% per attached location ± relations ± situations, with locally-produced goods cheap (and worthless to sell back) and distant goods at 150%/110%. Players can reverse-engineer and exploit this, which IS the trade gameplay — avoid opaque random pricing.
- Three standing tracks beat one: professional fame (Renown → pay/unlocks, decays 2-3/day), per-settlement friendship (Relations → prices, drifts to neutral ~0.25/day), and morality (Reputation). But Reputation is a cautionary tale: the wiki bluntly notes it currently does almost nothing (one contract-type gate at 80+). Don't ship a tracked stat without payoff — players notice.
- Decay everything that represents standing: renown and relations both erode daily, making fame a maintained resource and giving idle time a cost (alongside wages and food spoilage). Multiple soft clocks keep the sandbox from being safely turtle-able.
- Hidden push-your-luck meters make menus fun: haggling's Annoyance (random 3-6 per attempt, bust at 9, fail chance = 10%×accumulated) creates a one-safe/two-risky/three-impossible rhythm. But its per-head and advance-pay options lose money to rounding — the wiki calls them 'basically scams'. If you offer negotiation choices, make each at least situationally correct, and watch integer rounding in money reallocation.
- Snapshot-based goal eligibility is a documented pain point: ambitions are only checked when the selection screen opens, and ~15 of them get permanently auto-completed WITHOUT rewards if already satisfied (e.g., already having 12 men kills Roster of 12's reward). Evaluate goal conditions continuously or grant rewards retroactively; players keep helmets unsold and delay hires to game this — pure friction.
- Foreshadow your endgame escalation: crises are chosen-or-random at start, telegraphed at day 50-70, begin day 80-100, gated on player readiness (minimum days AND roster strength), last 30-100 days, never repeat consecutively, and require 50+ days of peace between. Optional 'permanent destruction' lets players opt into a world that can be irreversibly lost — stakes as a setting, not a default.
- Crises should attack different systems: Noble War locks map access and trade routes (political/economic pressure, world survives), Undead/Greenskin invasions destroy settlements (existential pressure), Holy War contests fixed objectives. Varying WHAT is threatened (access vs. existence vs. objectives) matters more than varying enemy skins.
- Tie strategic failure to tactical state via one variable: mood (0-100, trends to 50) converts unpaid wages and hunger into worse starting combat morale (Euphoric = 75% chance Confident; Angry caps at Breaking + desertion risk). One legible bridge stat beats many invisible penalties.
- Background-conditional events turn stat templates into characters: events keyed to backgrounds/traits/pairs (Brawler vs Brawler, Aging Swordmaster) plus background-specific retirement epilogues make the roster narratively sticky at low content cost. The wiki also warns its own event docs drifted from game code after DLC patches — events keyed to many conditions are a maintenance/correctness burden.
- Score formula encodes values: retirement score divides by days elapsed and multiplies 1.25× per crisis ended — explicitly rewarding speed and risk over safe grinding. Decide what play pattern you want to celebrate and put it in the formula.
- Consumables as friction, not bookkeeping: 4 supply types each map to one failure mode (no food → desertion spiral; no tools → gear breaks permanently; no medicine → injuries never heal; no ammo → ranged units idle), with auto-consumption (eat nearest-to-spoil first, auto-repair at 3 durability/hour) handling the tedium. Retinue followers then sell quality-of-life as progression (Quartermaster +50 caps, Blacksmith prevents weapon loss).
- Difficulty knobs should be orthogonal and honest: combat difficulty (spawn timing/size, ±5 to-hit on Beginner only), economic difficulty (pay −10%/step, sell prices, resource caps), and starting funds are independent, and enemy stats/shop stock never change — preserving learnable combat math at every setting.
- Procedural generation needs guaranteed structure: maps always get ~17 settlements in fixed type ratios and houses always get unique heraldry/leaders, but location placement and attached-location mixes vary — enough constancy to plan builds around, enough variance that trade routes and recruit availability differ per run (e.g., some maps are simply bad for trading, which the wiki flags as making trade 'an unreliable source of income' — a known cost of this approach).

## Community consensus & mod-scene analysis

SUMMARY: Community consensus (Steam forums, r/BattleBrothers commentary, dev forums, mod sites) holds that Battle Brothers' staying power comes from emergent storytelling: a procedurally generated low-fantasy sandbox where permadeath mercenaries with distinct backgrounds/traits create player-authored narratives, layered over deep tactical combat and a company-management economy. The loudest frustrations are RNG that feels punishing at the extremes despite being statistically fair, no mid-battle/exit saving (a hard dev refusal), a late game that plateaus into trivial repetition once bros hit the level-11 cap and top gear, samey contract templates, and chronic under-surfacing of information (hit math, settlement effects, recruit stats). The mod scene confirms these wants: the dominant overhauls (Legends, Reforged) add alternate starts, bigger/deeper perk systems, more enemy variety and endgame challenge, while the most-installed QoL mods almost all just expose hidden information or speed the game up.

## Permadeath roster with procedurally generated character identity
Every recruit is generated with a named background (farmhand, hedge knight, thief...), a backstory blurb, hidden stats, random traits, and talent stars that boost level-up rolls in 3 random attributes. Death in battle is permanent (corpses can even return as undead enemies). Characters accumulate permanent injuries and event history over a campaign.
WHY: This is the #1 cited reason for compulsion/replayability: players form attachments, mourn losses, and retell 'bro stories.' Identity + permanence converts tactical battles into emergent narrative. Community motto: 'losing is fun.'

## Procedural sandbox world with no scripted main plot + timed late-game crises
Each new campaign generates a new world map, settlements, factions, and contract flow. There is no main quest, only player-driven goals. Around a timer, a 'late game crisis' triggers (noble civil war, greenskin invasion, or undead scourge) that reshapes the world (towns can be permanently destroyed) and spawns themed contracts. Enemy difficulty scales with elapsed time but the scaling stops around day 267.
WHY: Procedural worlds + no handholding create the 'living world that goes on without you' feeling players praise. But the crisis system is the weakest-reviewed part: crisis contracts play like ordinary contracts, ending slides ignore player accomplishments, rewards are just gold, and post-crisis the difficulty plateau makes everything trivial except legendary locations (which are far too hard) — a binary difficulty gap that kills late-game engagement.

## d100 hit-roll tactical combat with stacked situational modifiers
Hex-grid, turn-based combat ordered by initiative. Attacks resolve as melee/ranged skill vs defense plus modifiers (surround bonus per adjacent ally, height advantage, weapon skill bonuses, shield/shieldwall, fatigue management, two-layer armor: helmet/body vs HP). Morale checks cause units to waver/flee. The combat log shows only bare roll results (e.g. 'Roll fail (dice 90)') without the modifier breakdown.
WHY: The tactical depth (risk management, positioning, fatigue economy) is the praised core. But unmitigated extreme rolls — missing three 81% attacks in a row, enemies landing sub-20% shots, a surrounded enemy dodging six attackers — is the single most common rage point. The opaque log makes it feel rigged even when fair; mods like 'Better Hit Information', 'Better Combat Log', and 'Less extreme hit rolls' exist purely to fix feel and transparency.

## Contract + renown + arms-dealer economy
The company takes procedurally generated contracts (kill X, escort caravan, deliver goods, patrol) negotiated for pay; renown gates better contracts. Real income, per veterans, comes from looting enemy weapons/armor, repairing them, and selling to wealthy towns. Daily wages, food, tools, and medicine create constant upkeep pressure. Settlement 'situations' (raided, prosperous...) alter local prices and availability.
WHY: Upkeep pressure forces the 'one more contract' loop and makes economic decisions matter. Criticisms: contract templates are too few and samey ('all the same: kill x, travel to x, protect x'), and the loot-resale meta is undiscoverable for new players. The best-remembered contracts are ones with mid-contract twists/ambushes — that's where the template system shines.

## Pre-battle autosave only; no saving during combat
The game autosaves before each battle; there is no manual save, no save-on-exit, during combat. Devs explicitly refused mid-battle saving to prevent save-scumming and preserve consequence (Ironman mode goes further). Battles can run 20-40 minutes.
WHY: Protects the permadeath/consequence philosophy, but is a perennial complaint: 'life happens' and players lose 20-40 minutes. The community-proposed compromise — a suspend-style save-on-exit that deletes on load — preserves integrity while respecting time; its absence generates lasting resentment and negative reviews.

## Level-11 cap with perk picks and build archetypes
Bros gain XP to level 11 max (veteran levels after give tiny stat gains, no perks), choosing 1 perk per level from a shared perk grid (e.g. Student, Recover, Nimble vs Battle Forged armor metas) plus stat rolls boosted by talent stars. Builds differentiate tank/two-hander/archer/hybrid roles.
WHY: Build-crafting is a major replayability driver, but the hard cap means a maxed roster stops growing, directly feeding late-game boredom ('all max level, top gear, just grinding for legendaries'). 'Uncapped Perk Points' is a popular mod; some perks being near-mandatory (Student/Recover) narrows perceived build variety.

## Blind recruitment gamble with paid tryouts
Recruits show only background, price, and flavor text; actual stats/traits/talents are hidden until hired. A 'tryout' fee partially reveals traits before purchase. Expensive backgrounds (hedge knight) can still roll badly.
WHY: Deliberate uncertainty that creates stories ('the beggar who became a champion') but a large faction calls it artificial difficulty — wasting 5k gold on a dud feels terrible. The 'Clever Recruiter' mod (full stat reveal pre-hire) is consistently popular, showing many players want the gamble removed or softened.

## Company origins (starting scenarios) as replayability multiplier
DLC-added origins (e.g. Lone Wolf, Cultists, Peasant Militia, Trading Caravan) change starting roster, resources, special rules, and sometimes max party size, reshaping the whole campaign's constraints.
WHY: Cheap-to-build but huge replay value — community consistently lists origin variety among reasons for 100s of hours. Validated by mods: Legends' six spell-casting alternate-start classes and 'Dark Age' scenario pack are among the most loved mod features.

## Named items and legendary locations as endgame chase content
Unique 'named/famed' weapons and armor with randomized superior stats spawn in fixed-map legendary locations and as rare rewards; legendary locations hold extremely hard boss-style fights (e.g. Black Monolith).
WHY: The only real endgame carrot in vanilla. Players grind for them but complain the difficulty jump to legendary locations is too steep relative to plateaued regular content; 'Craft Named Weapons' mod shows players want agency over this RNG chase.

## Legends overhaul mod feature set (what the biggest mod adds)
Alternate starts with player avatars/commanders and spellcasting classes (Warlock, Seer, Ranger, Berserker...), camping system with crafting/training/healing tents, world-generation options, rosters up to 27 fielded, benching mercs without consuming roster slots, per-background randomized perk trees (~51 perks each, half class-guaranteed half random), seven-layer armor, 4-skull contracts, XP for non-combat contracts, new enemies/factions/events.
WHY: A map of unmet vanilla demand: more start variety, downtime activities between battles, bigger rosters, deeper perk diversity, harder endgame contracts, and XP for non-combat play. Equally instructive: its top criticism is wrecked balance ('Shieldmaiden 30 base defense at level 1', 20+ units trivializing encounters) — 'it falls victim to its brilliance and ambition ruining the balance.'

## Reforged overhaul mod feature set (the balance-focused alternative)
Dynamic perk trees, 100+ new perks, a dynamic enemy spawn system, reworked enemy factions (Ancient Dead, Brigands, Nobles) with their own perks/abilities, new combat mechanics ('Reach', 'Crowded'), new equipment, nested tooltips, and QoL — explicitly aiming at depth-with-balance rather than content bloat.
WHY: Shows the community's second wave of demand after Legends: not more power, but more enemy variety, smarter spawning (fighting the same compositions gets stale), deeper perk choice, and better information (nested tooltips).

## QoL mod cluster = catalog of base-game friction
Most-recommended small mods: Swifter/Quicker (speed up world map + combat animations, scale event frequency to game speed), Better Hit Information (show full modifier breakdown per attack), Better Combat Log (morale checks logged, log not collapsed), Settlement Situations MSU (exact numeric effects in tooltips), End's Inventory Management Overhaul (favoriting, sell-protection, item data), Additional Keybinds (hotkeys for bag slots, rotation, shieldwall), Clever Recruiter (reveal recruit stats), 18 Bros in Battle (larger battles with scaled contracts), Sell Loot for More, Event Frequency control, Cart Upgrade (more inventory slots).
WHY: Nearly every popular QoL mod either (a) surfaces hidden math/state in-game, or (b) lets the player control pacing. This is the clearest community verdict on the base game's UI/feel deficits — players otherwise need a wiki open.

- Build the compulsion loop around emergent narrative, not scripted story: procedural world + permadeath + characters with generated identities (names, backgrounds, traits, scars, history) is what players cite for 100s-1000s of hours. Make every unit a potential story protagonist so losses hurt and victories are memorable.
- Permadeath/consequence design needs an attachment engine to work: backgrounds, traits, talent stars, and permanent injuries are cheap systems that convert generic units into 'bros' players mourn. Without them, permadeath is just attrition.
- Statistically fair RNG still feels rigged at the extremes. Mitigate feel without removing risk: show the full hit-chance modifier breakdown, log every roll transparently, and consider bounded/weighted rolls or streak-breakers (the existence of 'Less extreme hit rolls for the player' and 'Better Hit Information' mods proves demand). An opaque combat log ('Roll fail (dice 90)') turns variance into perceived cheating.
- Respect player time even in a consequences-matter game: a suspend-style save-on-exit (deleted on resume) preserves ironman integrity while letting players quit a 30-minute battle when life happens. The devs' flat refusal of any in-combat save is one of the most persistent sources of resentment — don't conflate anti-save-scumming with anti-pausing-your-life.
- Don't let difficulty plateau before the player's appetite does: enemy scaling that stops (day 267) plus a hard level/perk cap creates a dead zone where regular content is trivial and the only remaining challenges (legendary locations) are brutally out of reach. Provide a continuous late-game difficulty ladder (e.g. 4-skull elite contracts, scaling threats) and some form of continued progression.
- Make climax events mechanically and emotionally distinct: late-game crises were criticized for playing exactly like normal contracts and for giving zero recognition (identical ending slides, NPCs dismissing your deeds, gold-only rewards). Endgame events need unique mechanics, variable outcomes tied to player renown/actions, and non-monetary rewards.
- Contract/quest templates wear thin fast ('kill X, escort X, deliver X'); the missions players remember are ones with mid-contract twists and complications. Invest template budget in twist/event layers rather than more base templates.
- Surface all information in-game: the most popular mods just expose hidden numbers (settlement situation effects, hit math, recruit stats, morale checks). If players need a wiki or a mod to understand your systems, the UI is failing — nested/explicit tooltips should be native.
- Give players pacing control: separate speed sliders for world-map travel and combat animations (Swifter/Quicker mods) are near-universal installs; slow forced pacing reads as disrespect for time after the first 50 hours.
- Starting-scenario variety (origins) is the cheapest replayability multiplier: different starting rosters/resources/rules reshape the entire campaign. Both the official DLC origins and Legends' alternate-start classes are among the most-praised content per development effort.
- Deliberate information-gambles (blind recruitment) split the community: they generate stories but also feel like artificial difficulty when a 5k-gold hire rolls badly. Offer graduated reveal (paid tryouts exist in vanilla, mods reveal everything) and consider letting players buy more certainty rather than making it all-or-nothing.
- Beware content bloat in expansions/overhauls: Legends is loved for variety but criticized as 'completely wacked' on balance (overpowered starts, 20+ unit armies trivializing fights, non-scaling bosses) — 'it falls victim to its brilliance and ambition.' Reforged's positioning (fewer, balance-disciplined additions, enemy variety, dynamic spawns) reflects the lesson: players ultimately want curated depth over raw quantity.
- Downtime systems add texture: Legends' camping (crafting/training/healing between battles) and benching mercs without losing roster slots are flagged by players as things that 'MUST be in vanilla' — roster management friction and dead travel time are wasted design space.
- Onboarding matters even for a deliberately harsh game: newcomers bounce off the early grind (8 hours of level-1 contracts, not knowing the loot-resale economy, not knowing why archers miss). The 'no handholding' sandbox feel is core to the appeal, but key economic/tactical heuristics should be discoverable in-game rather than tribal knowledge.
- Reward non-combat play: escort/delivery/discovery contracts giving no XP in vanilla pushed players toward pure combat grinding; Legends adding XP to these contracts shows players want all play styles to advance their company.

## Developer postmortems & successor games

SUMMARY: Battle Brothers (Overhype Studios, Hamburg) began pre-2013 as a three-person hobby project to make "a medieval X-COM," blending XCOM/Jagged Alliance tactical combat with Mount & Blade's open mercenary-company sim; Early Access (April 2015) let the team go full-time and the game shipped 1.0 in March 2017 on a custom C++ engine scripted in Squirrel. Dev blogs and interviews show its defining traits — low-fantasy grounding, no main plot (procedural sandbox with "greater evil" end-game crises), bust-style character art, and permadeath-friendly low-variance RNG — were mostly deliberate responses to a tiny team's constraints and a replayability-first philosophy. Successors split the formula: Wartales softened RNG/permadeath and added a fixed handcrafted world (well received by players who bounced off BB's brutality), while Overhype's own follow-up Menace keeps the procedural DNA but fixes BB's punishing early game and lack of authored characters.

## Two-layer campaign structure (strategic world map + tactical hex battles)
A real-time-with-pause overworld where the player runs a mercenary company Mount & Blade-style (contracts, economy, travel, recruiting) feeds into turn-based tactical battles on small hex maps. The pitch was explicitly 'medieval X-COM': per developer Jan Taaks, the team built it because no one else had, drawing on X-COM/UFO Defense, Jagged Alliance, Mount & Blade and Heroes of Might & Magic (Turn Based Lovers '10 Turns' interview; PC Gamer called it 'turn-based Mount & Blade meets XCOM').
WHY: The strategic layer gives stakes and attachment to disposable tactical units; the tactical layer gives the strategic economy teeth. This loop is the genre template Wartales and others copied.

## Procedural sandbox instead of overarching plot
Every campaign generates a new world map, factions, and contracts. There is no linear story; instead each campaign is 'loosely framed within a narrative related to one of several greater evils' (late-game crises like greenskin invasion or undead scourge) whose source the player can eventually destroy, plus optional non-linear milestones (Ambitions system, Dev Blog #89). Dev Blog #22 FAQ: 'a completely open world which doesn't restrict the player to go anywhere based on story progress, only on being able to survive,' and explicitly no 'forced and repetitive story missions that conflict with our open world gameplay or get in the way of replayability.'
WHY: Devs argued a linear story 'would quickly conflict with replayability' and feel artificial against the player-authored mercenary fantasy. The 'greater evil' crisis gives each run an arc and ending without authored quests — emergent stories about your own named men replace a scripted plot.

## Low-fantasy, grounded setting
An 'unnamed middle european medieval low fantasy world wherein magic exists, but is rare' — no elves/dwarves/halflings (Dev Blog #25 FAQ). The original concept was wilder (aliens landing in medieval times, mistaken for folklore creatures) and was deliberately dropped. Player characters get no 'unrealistic and superpowery' skills; the devs 'keep things down to low power fantasy and promote a certain asymmetry between the player and the opponents' — humans stay grounded while monsters (orcs, undead, geists) carry the exotic abilities.
WHY: Grounding makes losses feel earned and tactical logic legible (spears, shields, armor work as expected), while asymmetric enemy design preserves variety. It also keeps human-vs-human combat the readable baseline.

## Bust/figurine character art (no legs, no full sprites)
The team started with full-body Flash animations and abandoned them: 'We are a team of just three people (only one of which is an artist!)' and showing every piece of equipped gear (exact helmet, armor, shield, weapon) on full bodies would have multiplied workload. Solution inspired by Unity of Command: detailed character busts on faction-colored sockets, like 'somewhat abstracted figurines' on miniature tabletop terrain, plus floating weapon icons so loadout is readable without per-weapon poses (Dev Blog #5). The cute big-headed proportions are intentional: 'the smaller a figure gets the more iconic and readable it has to be,' and the contrast with the grim setting means 'even the experience of losing can be fun.'
WHY: Turned a resource constraint into a visual signature, kept all gear visible (crucial for an equipment-driven game), and ensured readability at any zoom. Side-effect on mechanics: facing/flanking was cut partly because it 'would have needed character busts for every one of six possible directions' (Dev Blog #22).

## Custom engine: C++ core + Squirrel scripting + HTML UI
Custom C++ engine with nearly all gameplay written in Squirrel script (compiled to .cnut files, partially XXTEA-encrypted), and the UI originally rendered via Awesomium (HTML/JS), later dropped when Awesomium died. Confirmed via Steam dev posts/forums and Adam Milazzo's mod-kit writeup.
WHY: Script-heavy architecture made the game deeply moddable even without official tools — the community decrypted .cnut files and built a mod kit and script-hook ecosystem, which extended the game's life for years. The dead-middleware UI dependency is a cautionary tale.

## Low-variance RNG / 'beatable from the start' balancing
Hit-chance RNG exists (XCOM-style to-hit rolls) but extreme swings are flattened: e.g., crits were redesigned as a predictable '25% base chance to hit the head instead of the body for 50% extra damage.' Stated principle (Dev Blog #22): avoid 'game mechanics that boil down to pure luck and which can be beat simply by reloading. Everything should be beatable with the right strategy from the start.'
WHY: Makes permadeath and ironman feel fair — variance is bounded enough that good play dominates over enough rolls, so deaths read as consequences rather than dice screwjobs.

## Individual permadeath with company-level resilience + Ironman mode
Dead brothers are gone permanently and 'most Battle Brothers will die before having the chance to grow old,' but losing most of your men doesn't end the campaign — the company itself is the persistent entity and recovery is designed to be possible. No saving mid-combat ('does not fit our design goals'); Ironman is save-on-exit only, while normal mode allows free saves. Devs acknowledged save-scumming can't be fully prevented: 'it really is up to players to discipline themselves' — play ironman at lower difficulty instead.
WHY: Two-tier loss design (units expendable, campaign resilient) is what makes permadeath emotionally heavy but not run-ending; offering ironman as opt-in respects both player types without compromising the core design.

## Procedurally generated character identity
Every recruit gets a procedurally generated backstory/origin (farmhand, hedge knight, cripple…) that modifies combat stats and traits (Dev Blog #25), plus generated names, faces, hidden traits revealed through play, and permanent injuries/scars.
WHY: Cheap (text + stat templates) but creates the attachment that powers the permadeath drama — the game's stories are about these generated men, substituting for the absent authored narrative.

- Constraints can become identity: the iconic bust art exists because 3 people (1 artist) couldn't animate full bodies showing all equipment; leaning into a tabletop-figurine framing made the compromise feel intentional rather than cheap (Dev Blog #5).
- Decide early between replayability and authored story — Overhype's position is they're in direct conflict: a linear plot 'gets in the way of replayability,' so they shipped a sandbox with a loose 'greater evil' crisis arc and optional Ambitions milestones for direction. Players still perennially ask for a campaign mode, so expect that criticism if you go pure sandbox.
- Be willing to kill your premise: the original 'aliens in medieval times' concept was dropped for grounded low fantasy; the devs explicitly warn that 'being too strict with the concepts and mechanics of a game can be a very bad thing.'
- A script-layer architecture (C++ core, Squirrel gameplay) gave huge modding longevity for free — but shipping no official mod tools and relying on dying middleware (Awesomium for UI) created avoidable pain.
- Permadeath needs two designs at once: bounded RNG ('everything should be beatable with the right strategy', predictable crit math, no pure-luck mechanics) and a resilient meta-layer (the company survives a wiped roster). Anti-savescum should be solved by design and opt-in ironman, not technical lockouts.
- Timeline/team reality check: hobby project from ~2013, Greenlight Oct 2014, Early Access April 2015 (funded going full-time), 1.0 March 2017, then ~8 years of DLC/updates (latest fixes 2025) — all with a core of 3 (Jan: biz/PR, Paul: art, Christof: code) plus freelancers like writer Casey. Long Early Access plus long post-launch support is the indie tactics-game playbook.
- Known pain point the devs themselves now admit: the brutal early game. For their successor Menace they inverted the curve ('easy at the start then get harder') because BB's hardest-at-the-start difficulty was 'frustrating for players,' and they added authored, multi-layered characters — 'impossible in Battle Brothers' — while keeping the procedural, play-your-own-way core.
- Wartales shows the demand for a softer take: fixed handcrafted map and storyline, largely deterministic attack outcomes (less to-hit RNG), optional/lenient death, rich out-of-combat sim (camping, crafting, fishing, mining), full 3D characters. Community reception credits it with better exploration/immersion and accessibility, while conceding Battle Brothers retains deeper tactical builds, tighter battles, and higher challenge — they coexist rather than one replacing the other.
- Sands of Salzaar took the Mount & Blade mercenary frame in the opposite direction (real-time Diablo-style combat, Chinese fantasy, overarching story) and was received as fun but 'janky'; it confirms Battle Brothers' moat is tactical depth, not the mercenary-company wrapper itself.
- If flanking/facing matters to your design, budget art for it from day one — BB cut directional facing mechanics because the bust style would have required 6-direction art the team couldn't afford.

## Tech stack & architecture research

SUMMARY: Recommendation: Godot 4 (4.4+/4.6-era, 2026) with statically-typed GDScript as the primary language, optionally adding C# for hot computational paths later. Rationale: (1) The developer is an experienced Python programmer but not a game developer — GDScript is deliberately Python-like (indentation, dynamic-feeling syntax, dicts/lists), so productivity arrives in days, not months; (2) Godot is 2D-first with a dedicated 2D renderer, pixel-coordinate system, TileMapLayer, and — critically for a Battle Brothers-like — the best built-in UI toolkit of any engine (Control nodes with containers/themes; the Godot editor itself is built with it), which matters enormously for a UI-heavy management/tactics game; (3) free, MIT-licensed, no royalties, no policy-rug risk — relevant given Unity's 2023 Runtime Fee trust damage that still lingers in 2026 even after cancellation; (4) proven commercial 2D track record (Backpack Battles ~$5.2M, Dome Keeper ~$6.1M, Brotato; multiple shipped tactics titles) and a mature mod-loading ecosystem (Godot Mod Loader, .pck/.zip resource-pack loading) used by shipped moddable games. Unity is the credible runner-up (bigger asset store, console ports, mature C# tooling) but is heavier, 3D-first in workflow, and overkill here. MonoGame/LÖVE give maximal control but force you to build UI, scene tooling, and editors yourself — exactly the wrong trade for a first-time game dev whose game is 70% UI and systems. Phaser/PixiJS+Electron/Tauri ships on Steam but Steam SDK integration is third-party/fragile and desktop perf tuning is on you. Python+pygame is fine for throwaway combat-rules prototypes (the dev's home turf) but a dead end for shipping this scope. A custom engine (the actual Battle Brothers route: C++ + SFML + Squirrel scripting + Awesomium HTML UI) cost two experienced programmers years and is not advisable for a solo non-gamedev in 2026. Key architectural stance regardless of engine: keep the simulation (combat rules, world state, RNG) as a pure, engine-agnostic, deterministic core in plain code/data, with Godot scenes acting only as a view layer — this gives testability (unit-test rules headlessly), determinism, save/load simplicity, and moddability almost for free.

## Godot 4 — GDScript-first (C# optional for hot paths) [RECOMMENDED]
FIT: Best overall fit. 2D-first engine with a separate 2D pipeline, pixel coordinates, TileMap/TileMapLayer with custom-shaped (hex) tiles, built-in AStar2D/AStar3D and AStarGrid2D pathfinding, and the strongest built-in UI system of any engine — decisive for a Battle Brothers-like where roster management, inventory, perk trees, contracts, and tooltips are half the game. GDScript is Python-like, so an experienced Python/ML programmer is productive almost immediately; typed GDScript closes most of the performance gap (~59% faster than untyped) and game logic is engine-API-bound anyway. Free/MIT, ~150MB editor, near-instant script reload (0.31s vs Unity's ~15s domain reload) — fast iteration loop suits deep-simulation tuning. Mod support is a solved pattern: ProjectSettings.load_resource_pack() for .zip/.pck content packs plus the community Godot Mod Loader (used by shipped games Brotato, Dome Keeper, Windowkill) with Steam Workshop/Thunderstore support. Commercial 2D/tactics proof: Backpack Battles (~$5.2M), Dome Keeper (~$6.1M), Lancer Tactics, multiple Steam tactics titles. GMTK jam share went 19%→37%→~40% (2024–2025), so community/tutorial volume is now on par with Unity for indies.
PROS: GDScript is Python-like: shortest possible learning curve for this specific developer; productive in hours-to-days; Best-in-class built-in UI toolkit (Control nodes, containers, themes, rich text) — critical for this UI-heavy genre; True 2D-first: dedicated 2D renderer, pixel coordinates, TileMap with hex-capable custom tile shapes, built-in A* pathfinding; Free, open-source (MIT), zero royalties, no licensing-policy risk; engine source is forkable if ever needed; Very fast iteration: tiny editor, sub-second script reload, scene-based hot testing of individual battle/UI scenes; Mature modding path: load_resource_pack(.pck/.zip), Resource.take_over_path script overrides, community Godot Mod Loader with Workshop support, proven in shipped games; Seeded determinism supported: RandomNumberGenerator class with explicit seed and state properties, multiple independent streams; C# escape hatch exists in the same engine if a simulation hot path ever needs it (procgen, AI search); Proven commercial 2D/tactics titles shipped; large and fast-growing community (≈40% of GMTK 2025 jam entries)
CONS: GDScript tooling (refactoring, static analysis) is weaker than C#/Rider for 50k+ line codebases — mitigate with typed GDScript, small files, and an engine-agnostic core; Console porting requires third-party partners (W4 Games, etc.) — fine for PC-first, a cost if console matters later; C# in Godot is second-class in docs/addons (only ~16% of Godot devs use it); mixing languages adds friction; Built-in Resource serialization is convenient but unsafe for untrusted files (resources can embed scripts) — must use JSON/store_var or sanitized loading for saves and mods; Fewer ready-made genre assets/plugins than Unity's Asset Store (though for deep-sim games most code is bespoke anyway)

## Unity (C#)
FIT: Capable runner-up; everything needed is achievable, and C# is a fine language for a big simulation codebase. But Unity is 3D-first in workflow (2D is a layer over the 3D pipeline), the editor is heavy with slow domain reloads (~15s), UI requires choosing between aging uGUI and still-maturing UI Toolkit, and built-in moddability is poor (mods generally need Harmony/BepInEx-style hacks or a custom asset pipeline). The 2023 Runtime Fee was cancelled (reverted to seat-based pricing, Personal free under $200k revenue), and Unity 6.4 shipped in March 2026 under new leadership, but indie trust recovery is still 'cautiously watching' and the editor/iteration weight is real friction for a solo dev tuning a simulation daily.
PROS: Largest asset store and tutorial corpus; abundant tactics/grid plugins and contractors; C# + Rider/Visual Studio is excellent for large codebases; NuGet ecosystem; Best-in-class console/platform porting if scope ever expands beyond PC; Burst/Jobs available if simulation perf ever becomes a real bottleneck
CONS: Heavy editor, ~15s script-reload domain cycles — slow iteration for daily sim/UI tuning; 2D and UI stories are fragmented (uGUI vs UI Toolkit; 2D rides on 3D pipeline); Closed source, seat-based licensing, and residual post-Runtime-Fee trust concerns; policy risk is non-zero; Moddability is not built-in; data-driven ScriptableObjects don't load from user folders without custom work (Addressables/AssetBundles complexity); C# learning curve plus Unity-specific architecture (MonoBehaviours, prefabs, serialization quirks) is a double curve for a non-gamedev

## MonoGame (C#)
FIT: Wrong trade-off for this developer. MonoGame is a code-first XNA-descendant framework (Stardew Valley, Celeste) that is healthy in 2026 (funded by ConcernedApe through ~2028, 3.8.5 in progress, NativeAOT backend landing), and turn-based games don't need engine features like physics. But it ships no editor, no scene system, no UI toolkit (community 'Gum' UI is improving but young), and no content tooling beyond the aging MGCB pipeline (replacement planned for 4.0). For a Battle Brothers-like, the developer would spend the first 6–12 months building UI widgets, tooltips, layout, save tooling, and editors instead of the game — while also learning C# and game architecture from scratch.
PROS: Total architectural control; nothing fights your deterministic, data-driven core; Proven for exactly this style of 2D PC game (Stardew Valley); tiny runtime, easy Steam shipping; Healthy 2026 status: funded 24+ months, active board, NativeAOT support arriving; C# is solid for large simulation codebases
CONS: No editor, no UI system, no scene tools — months of yak-shaving before gameplay, fatal for a UI-heavy genre; Content pipeline (MGCB) is dated; replacement not landed yet; Much smaller community and fewer learning resources than Godot/Unity; Developer must learn C# AND build engine-level infrastructure simultaneously while inexperienced in gamedev

## LÖVE / love2d (Lua)
FIT: Charming and genuinely viable for shipped 2D indies (Balatro is the flagship 2026-era proof; Moonring is a LÖVE RPG), and Lua is trivial for a Python dev. But like MonoGame it has no editor and no UI library of consequence — immediate-mode community libs (SUIT, etc.) won't scale to Battle Brothers-density UI without heavy custom work. Lua's weak typing and 1-based arrays grate at 50k+ lines of simulation code. Best thought of as a framework for small-to-mid scope games, not deep-sim tactics RPGs, unless the developer enjoys building infrastructure.
PROS: Extremely simple, fast startup; Lua learnable by a Python dev in a day; LuaJIT performance is excellent for simulation loops; Free, tiny, easy Steam distribution; commercial proof exists (Balatro); Lua doubles as a natural mod-scripting language (the Battle Brothers approach used Squirrel similarly)
CONS: No editor, no scene system, no serious UI toolkit — the genre's biggest cost center is unsupported; Dynamic, untyped Lua at large codebase scale is a maintainability hazard (no good static tooling vs typed GDScript/C#); Small ecosystem; you write tilemap/hex/pathfinding/save infrastructure yourself; Mobile/console paths are weak (irrelevant for PC-first, but limits options)

## Phaser or PixiJS + TypeScript (Electron/Tauri shell for Steam)
FIT: Workable but awkward for PC-first Steam. TypeScript is a fine language and HTML/CSS is ironically great for heavy UI (Battle Brothers itself used Awesomium HTML UI). Real games ship this way (Desktop Heroes, Phaser+Electron, Steam Oct 2025; Phaser's own 2025 Electron publishing guides; Tauri gives ~100MB smaller bundles). But the Steam SDK has no official JS bindings — you depend on third-party greenworks/steamworks.js wrappers that are 'often outdated and poorly maintained' — and Electron isn't optimized for an always-running game, so memory/perf tuning is on you. The browser sandbox also complicates file-system-level modding.
PROS: TypeScript: strong typing + huge ecosystem; comfortable jump from Python; HTML/CSS UI is extremely productive for dense, data-rich interfaces; Web build for free demos/playtests in the browser; PixiJS rendering is fast for 2D sprite scenes
CONS: Steamworks integration relies on unofficial, poorly maintained JS wrappers; Electron bloat (~100MB+) and game-unfriendly resource behavior; Tauri smaller but webview inconsistencies across OSes; No editor/scene tooling; save files, modding from user folders, and native FS access all need shell-layer plumbing; Perception/perf ceiling for a long-session simulation game; GC pauses and webview quirks are your problem

## Python + pygame (prototyping only)
FIT: Not a shipping vehicle for this scope (distribution pain, performance ceiling, no UI/tooling), but a legitimate week-one tool for THIS developer: prototype the deterministic combat core — hex math, action economy, hit formulas, utility AI scoring — as a pure-Python library with pytest before any engine is involved. That library's design (entities as data, seeded RNG streams, command-pattern actions) then ports almost line-for-line to typed GDScript, which is syntactically Python-adjacent.
PROS: Zero learning curve for this developer; fastest possible path to validating game rules; pytest-driven development of combat rules and AI is natural and rigorous; Pure-logic prototype enforces the engine-agnostic-core discipline from day one; NumPy available for procgen experiments (noise, Voronoi via scipy.spatial)
CONS: Shipping Python games on Steam is painful (packaging, AV false positives, perf); pygame has no UI toolkit, no editor, weak text/UI rendering for dense interfaces; Risk of prototype-becomes-product trap; must treat it as disposable; Effectively no commercial track record in this genre at this scope

## Custom engine (C++/Rust + SDL/SFML/wgpu)
FIT: This is what Battle Brothers actually did — custom C++ engine with SFML, PhysicsFS, Squirrel for gameplay scripting, and Awesomium for HTML-driven UI — but Overhype was a multi-person team with experienced engine programmers, and even they spent years on it and later hired a dedicated UI developer. For a solo dev with no gamedev background it converts a 2–4 year game project into a 5+ year engine-plus-game project, with C++/Rust learning stacked on top. The only honest argument for it (total control, determinism, mod scripting layer) is fully achievable inside Godot by keeping the sim core engine-agnostic.
PROS: Total control over determinism, serialization, threading, and mod surface; No engine licensing or upgrade-treadmill risk ever; The historically authentic Battle Brothers route, proving the architecture works
CONS: Years of pre-gameplay infrastructure work (rendering, UI, audio, input, tooling, save systems, platform quirks); Requires C++/Rust mastery the developer does not have; debugging engine + game + new language simultaneously; UI from scratch is brutal — Battle Brothers resorted to embedding an HTML renderer and hiring a UI specialist; Highest project-failure risk of all options for a solo first-time game developer

- Engine-agnostic simulation core (the single most important decision): implement battle rules, world state, and AI as plain classes/data (in Godot: RefCounted/plain objects, NOT Nodes) with no rendering or scene-tree dependencies. Godot scenes subscribe to sim events and animate the results. This yields headless unit testing of combat rules (GUT/gdUnit4, or run logic via godot --headless), deterministic replays, simpler saves, and a clean mod API boundary. Battle Brothers itself separated C++ engine from Squirrel-scripted gameplay along exactly this seam.
- Hex grid math: use axial coordinates (q, r) as the canonical storage form, converting to cube coordinates (x+y+z=0) for algorithms — distance = max(|dx|,|dy|,|dz|), rotation, ring/spiral iteration, line-drawing via cube lerp + rounding. The definitive reference is Red Blob Games' hexagons guide and its implementation companion (code in Python, GDScript-portable, C#, TS): https://www.redblobgames.com/grids/hexagons/ . Pick ONE orientation (pointy-top vs flat-top) and offset parity early and never mix. Store the battle map as a Dictionary keyed by Vector2i(q,r) or a dense array for fixed-size maps; Godot's TileMapLayer natively supports hex tile shapes for rendering, and AStar2D handles weighted hex pathfinding out of the box (or write a 50-line A* over the sim's own graph to keep pathfinding inside the deterministic core).
- Data-driven content: define items, perks, skills, enemies, contracts, and events as data files, not code. In Godot the idiomatic options are custom Resources (.tres) for editor-integrated authoring vs JSON for mod-friendliness — recommended hybrid: JSON (or TOML) as the canonical moddable format loaded into typed registry objects at boot, with IDs as stable string keys ('perk.shield_expert'). Effects/abilities should be composed from small reusable components (stat modifiers, triggers, conditions) referenced by data — 'a tight, shallow group of classes representing objects through attributes' rather than a subclass per item. Hot-reload the registries in dev builds for fast balancing iteration. This single decision delivers both faster iteration AND moddability.
- Deterministic turn resolution and seeded RNG: use multiple named RNG streams (combat-rolls, AI-decisions, loot, worldgen, VFX) so cosmetic randomness never perturbs gameplay sequences — Godot's RandomNumberGenerator exposes seed and state for exactly this; never use the global randi() in sim code. Model player/AI actions as serializable command objects (Command pattern); turn resolution = fold(commands, state) as a pure function. Benefits: replay files = initial seed + command log; desync-style bug reports become reproducible; 'verify save integrity by re-simulating' is possible. Beware float nondeterminism only if you ever need cross-machine identical replays — integer math for combat formulas avoids it entirely (Battle Brothers-style percentage rolls are naturally integer).
- Save/load of a big world state: snapshot the engine-agnostic sim state, not the scene tree. Use explicit to_dict()/from_dict() (or versioned binary via FileAccess.store_var) per system; include a save-format version int from day one and write migration shims on change. SECURITY: do NOT load Godot Resources (.tres/.res) from user-writable save or mod directories — Godot resources can embed executable scripts; JSON/store_var(false) have no code-execution path. For a Battle Brothers-scale world (hundreds of settlements/parties, thousands of items), lazy strategies matter less than schema discipline: store entities in flat tables keyed by stable IDs with references-by-ID (never object pointers), which also makes saves diffable and mod-tolerant. Compress with Godot's built-in gzip; a full world snapshot at this scale is single-digit MB and saves in milliseconds.
- Utility-based tactical AI: score every (unit, action, target/tile) tuple with normalized 0–1 considerations multiplied together (expected damage, kill probability, retaliation risk, ally support, terrain/elevation bonus, morale impact), pick the argmax with a small temperature for variety. Canonical references: Dave Mark's Infinite Axis Utility System and 'Modular Tactical Influence Maps' (Game AI Pro 2, ch.30, free PDF: gameaipro.com) and Mike Lewis 'Choosing Effective Utility-Based Considerations' (Game AI Pro 3, ch.13). Layer influence maps over the hex grid (threat map = sum of enemy attack ranges/damage; support map = ally proximity) as cheap shared inputs to per-unit scoring — turn-based budgets are generous, so even exhaustive scoring of all reachable hexes per unit is fine. Make AI personality data-driven: archetype weight tables (cowardly, berserker, defensive) are just multiplier sets in the same JSON content system, which also makes AI moddable. Keep AI decisions inside the deterministic core using the dedicated AI RNG stream.
- Procedural overworld: the canonical technique for Battle Brothers-style overworlds is Amit Patel's polygonal map generation — Voronoi relaxation (Lloyd) over random points + Delaunay dual, elevation assigned from coast distance or noise, moisture/biome assignment per cell, rivers along Delaunay edges downhill: http://www-cs-students.stanford.edu/~amitp/game-programming/polygon-map-generation/ . Combine layered simplex/Perlin noise (broad landmass shape) with Voronoi regions (political territories, settlement placement, faction borders). Godot has FastNoiseLite built in; Voronoi/Delaunay via Geometry2D or a small port. Generate from the dedicated worldgen RNG stream so a world seed fully reproduces the map. Generate-then-bake: run generation once at campaign start and serialize the result into normal world state rather than regenerating from seed on load — simpler and migration-safe.
- Event system: use a global event-bus autoload (Godot signals on a singleton) for cross-cutting notifications (unit_died, contract_completed, settlement_raided), with the sim core emitting plain typed event objects that both the UI layer and content systems consume. Battle Brothers-style narrative events are pure data: trigger conditions (predicates over world state) + weighted outcomes + effect lists, evaluated by a generic event engine — this makes the event pool trivially moddable. Decouple sim events (deterministic, logged, part of replay) from presentation events (animations, sounds) — the UI replays sim events at presentation speed, which also gives you free 'fast combat' toggles.
- Moddability patterns (plan from day one, retrofit is painful): three tiers — (1) data mods: load JSON content packs from user://mods with load-order, ID-based override/merge semantics (new items/perks/enemies/events require zero code); (2) script mods: Godot's ProjectSettings.load_resource_pack(.pck/.zip) plugs mods into the virtual filesystem, and the community Godot Mod Loader (github.com/GodotModding/godot-mod-loader; used by Brotato, Dome Keeper) provides script extension/hooking, dependencies, and Steam Workshop/Thunderstore integration; (3) asset mods: sprite/sound replacement via the same resource-pack path overrides. Battle Brothers' own modding ecosystem (MSU mod kit) demonstrates the demand pattern: hooks into data definitions and script functions. Design rules that enable this: stable string IDs everywhere, registries instead of hardcoded enums, effects-as-composable-components, and a documented mod manifest with versioning.
- Suggested path for this developer: (week 0–4) prototype the pure combat core in Python with pytest — hex math, initiative, action economy, hit/armor formulas, basic utility AI — to validate fun and lock the rules schema; (month 2+) port the core to typed GDScript in Godot 4 (near-mechanical translation given the syntactic similarity), build one battle scene + UI vertical slice; defer the overworld until tactical combat is fun; adopt typed GDScript everywhere (--warnings-as-errors style strictness) to recover most static-typing safety; only reach for C# if profiling shows a real hot path (likely candidates: worldgen, AI search at large unit counts). Total cost of engine choice if wrong: low — the engine-agnostic core ports anywhere.

## Official site & dev blogs

SUMMARY: The official Battle Brothers site (Overhype Studios) pitches the game as a turn-based tactical RPG in which you lead a mercenary company of "regular human beings" through a gritty, low-power, procedurally generated medieval fantasy world, with no class system, permadeath, and equipment-driven tactics. The site's Features page, FAQ, and dev blogs (#145 The Anatomists, #146 The Oathtakers, both part of the free 2025 "Of Flesh and Faith" DLC) reveal a strongly opinionated design philosophy: the company is the protagonist (no player avatar, no story campaign, singleplayer only, no fantasy races), and replayability comes from procedural campaigns, origins with distinct rule-sets, and late-game crisis events rather than scripted narrative.

## Mercenary company management (strategic layer)
The player leads a mercenary company on a procedurally generated open-world map with free travel. You earn coin via a contract-based economy, loot battlefields, pursue fleeing enemies, resupply in towns, and hire new mercenaries. The company — not an individual hero — is the persistent entity; there is deliberately no player character on the battlefield.
WHY: Creates a 'manager of expendable men' fantasy: attachment is to the band and its survival, which makes permadeath tolerable and replayable rather than campaign-ending.

## Turn-based tactical combat with weapon-specific skills
Battles are turn-based and tactical, using historical equipment with distinct mechanics: axes split shields, maces stun, spears form spearwalls, warhammers crush armor. 'Equipment that matters. Different weapons grant unique skills' — a character's active abilities come largely from the weapon they hold. Enemies have diverse AI with their own unique equipment and skills.
WHY: Loadout choice becomes the tactical vocabulary; counters (e.g., bring axes vs. shield-walls) create rock-paper-scissors depth without a class system, and loot directly translates into new tactical options.

## Classless character progression with backgrounds, traits, and perks
Every recruit comes with a background story and traits (e.g., stuttering ratcatcher, greedy witch hunter, disowned drunken noble) that affect stats and events. 'Character development without a restrictive class-system': characters earn XP in combat, level up, and pick perks freely.
WHY: Procedurally flavored recruits generate emergent personal stories and make each hire a gamble/discovery; free perk picks make builds a core player-expression system.

## Permadeath plus a detailed injury system
'All characters that die in combat will stay dead – unless they return as the undead.' Combat inflicts brutal, detailed injuries (temporary and permanent) rather than simple HP loss. The FAQ explains a controllable player character was rejected because it 'would conflict too much with core gameplay elements such as permadeath of individual characters.'
WHY: Stakes and grit: every engagement risks irreversible loss, which drives tension, cautious tactics, and memorable war stories; injuries create a softer failure gradient below outright death.

## Late-game crises (procedural endgame)
Three large-scale late-game crisis events can engulf the procedurally generated world: a war between noble houses, a greenskin invasion, and an undead scourge.
WHY: Provides escalating endgame goals and world-shaking stakes in a sandbox without a scripted story campaign, giving each run a climax.

## Event system
Atmospheric text events fire during travel/camp, presenting decisions that hook into character backgrounds, traits, and company state.
WHY: Cheap-to-produce narrative texture that personalizes procedurally generated characters and breaks up map travel.

## Company origins as rule-set variants (DLC pattern)
DLC adds 'origins' that change the starting condition and ongoing rules of a campaign. Example from the free 'Of Flesh and Faith' DLC (March 2025): the Anatomists and the Oathtakers. New origin content requires starting a new campaign.
WHY: Replayability through asymmetric starts and modified rule-sets rather than just more content; each origin is effectively a new way to play the same sandbox.

## Oathtakers oath system (Dev Blog #146)
Replaces the normal long-term 'ambitions' system: every ten days the player must choose one of several oaths, each granting boons and burdens while active (e.g., Oath of Honor gives confident morale but bans all ranged/throwing weapons; Oath of Vengeance buffs attributes vs. greenskins but penalizes vs. others). Completing a ten-day oath cycle yields fixed renown, with bonus renown for difficulty-dependent objectives (e.g., killing opponents in one-on-one fights without interference). Twelve oaths total; an oath cannot be repeated consecutively. Origin starts with two skilled warriors, quality gear, a company banner on day one, and a relic replacing the sergeant's sash.
WHY: Rotating self-imposed constraints force the player to keep changing tactics and roster usage, converting buffs/debuffs into a periodic strategic decision and a renown-based progression loop.

## Anatomists dissection and mutation system (Dev Blog #145)
On first killing each type of non-human opponent, the company dissects the corpse and creates one potion unique to that enemy type; four ledgers (beasts, greenskins, undead, legendary creatures) track which opponents have been dissected, with legendary potions giving particularly powerful effects. Any squad member can drink a serum to permanently mutate for special effects (explicitly compared to The Witcher's mutations), accompanied by new story events and a unique banner. The origin starts with three mediocre fighters but high funds and unique equipment.
WHY: Turns the bestiary into a collection-driven progression system — 'incentivizes hunting down each and every type of opponent in the game' — rewarding exploration and combat variety, and offsets a weak starting roster with a long-term power fantasy of transcending human limits.

## Production-values support content
Two hours of orchestral soundtrack; 70 Steam achievements and trading cards; post-launch free updates (e.g., update 1.5.1 adding two new legendary locations); transmedia tie-in novel 'The Witch Hunter' by game writer Casey Hollingshead concluding the 'strange traveler' storyline.
WHY: Shows long-tail support and world-building investment that sustains community engagement years after release (game still receiving free content in 2025, with console ports of the DLC).

- Pick a tight fantasy and defend it ruthlessly: Overhype repeats that the game is about 'leading a mercenary company of regular human beings in a low fantasy world,' and uses that one sentence to justify cutting playable fantasy races, mages, a player avatar, base-building, and multiplayer ('built solely as a singleplayer game'); they even redirect players elsewhere ('we recommend Mount & Blade Warband') rather than dilute the design.
- Make systems mutually reinforcing: the absence of a player character is not an oversight but a requirement — a hero unit 'would conflict too much with... permadeath of individual characters.' Permadeath, classless procedurally generated recruits, and the company-as-protagonist frame all support each other; copying one without the others weakens the loop.
- Replace scripted story with procedural structure: a procedurally generated world plus contract economy plus three late-game crises plus a text-event system delivers narrative arc and climax without authored campaign content — a cheap, replayable alternative for a small team.
- Tie tactics to equipment, not classes: weapon-specific skills (axes split shields, maces stun, spears wall, hammers crush armor) make loot tactically meaningful and create counterplay depth without a class system.
- Use origins/rule-set variants as DLC: new starting setups that rewrite campaign rules (Oathtakers' rotating 10-day boon/burden oaths replacing ambitions; Anatomists' dissect-every-enemy-type potion collection) deliver high replay value per content dollar — but note the pain point that new origin content forces a new campaign ('you won't have access to all of the new content until you start a new campaign').
- Design constraint-based progression: the Oathtakers show that periodic self-imposed restrictions (e.g., 'cannot use any ranged weapons' in exchange for morale) are a progression mechanic in themselves, forcing tactical variety; banning consecutive repeats of the same oath prevents players from locking into one optimal constraint.
- Turn the bestiary into a checklist reward: Anatomists' first-kill dissection potions 'incentivize hunting down each and every type of opponent,' converting existing enemy variety into a new progression/exploration system at low content cost.
- Pain points to avoid: early scope decisions became permanent debt — 'we had to make tough decisions to get the game made at all. These decisions can't be undone anymore,' including no female characters (justified by a 250,000+ word localization burden) and no mod support, both deferred to a hypothetical sequel with 'full-fledged mod support.' Plan text volume, character-gender flexibility, and moddability into the architecture early.
- Long-tail goodwill: free DLC ('Of Flesh and Faith') and free updates with new legendary locations eight-plus years post-launch, plus a tie-in novel by the game's own writer, kept the community alive while the studio moved to its next game.

## Steam page & reception

SUMMARY: The Steam page presents Battle Brothers (Overhype Studios, released March 24, 2017; published with Hooded Horse) as a turn-based tactical RPG about leading a procedurally generated medieval mercenary company — real-time overworld travel plus hex-grid tactical battles, with classless character builds, permadeath, injuries, fatigue, and morale. Reception is Very Positive (89% of ~27,600 all-language reviews; recent ~82%), with praise centering on tactical depth, replayability, and 'fair' symmetric rules, and complaints about punishing RNG, a steep learning curve with little tutorial, and repetitive contract variety. Three paid expansions (Beasts & Exploration, Warriors of the North, Blazing Deserts) plus two free DLCs (Lindwurm, Of Flesh and Faith) extended the game for 5+ years; system requirements are tiny (2 GB RAM/storage, OpenGL 3.3, 2D engine, Windows only).

## Open-world strategic layer with real-time travel
The company travels in real time across a procedurally generated landscape, visiting settlements and strongholds spread across the realm. Settlements and Noble Houses offer dynamically generated contracts of varying difficulty and pay; how quests are accomplished determines relationships with factions.
WHY: Creates a sandbox campaign loop (travel -> contract -> battle -> resupply) where the world feels alive and reputation/faction standing gives consequences to player choices, driving emergent stories rather than scripted ones.

## Dynamic late-game crises
Various crises (e.g., wars, invasions) can ignite and 'fundamentally reshape the landscape, demanding radically different strategies and offering new opportunities.' Blazing Deserts DLC added an additional late-game crisis.
WHY: Provides escalating endgame pressure and replay variety in a sandbox that otherwise has no fixed story, preventing the mid/late game from going flat.

## Economy beyond contracts
Profitable trade between cities/settlements and treasure 'hidden in the wild' supplement contract income; the player pays ongoing salaries, food, and equipment costs for the roster.
WHY: Money as constant upkeep pressure makes the mercenary fantasy real — every casualty, repair, and hire has economic weight, and trade/exploration are alternative playstyles.

## Turn-based hex tactical combat with terrain
Battles play on hex grids ('turn-based, hexagonal' per PC Gamer) with rough terrain, height advantages, and obstacles that can be exploited. Enemies use the identical weapons, skills, and rules as the player (called out by reviewers as 'fair' design).
WHY: Terrain and symmetric rules make positioning the core skill and make outcomes feel earned; players can learn enemy capabilities by learning their own.

## Fatigue resource in combat
More powerful attacks and abilities exhaust warriors faster; fatigue accumulates per action and must be managed alongside action points.
WHY: Acts as a pacing/anti-spam resource — heavy armor and big swings cost stamina, creating a tradeoff between burst power and endurance over a long fight.

## Morale system
Morale is 'another key factor to manage on the battlefield' — units can waver, panic, or flee based on battlefield events (deaths, being surrounded, etc.).
WHY: Adds a psychological win condition (routing enemies) and a failure cascade risk for the player, rewarding intimidation tactics and protecting fragile recruits.

## Detailed injury and permadeath system
Wounds inflict temporary and permanent consequences (lost eyes, maimed limbs); dead mercenaries stay dead. Reviewers note permadeath creates strong emotional investment in individual soldiers.
WHY: Consequence persistence is the emotional engine of the game — attachment plus loss generates the war stories players cite in reviews.

## Classless character progression
'Does away with a restrictive class-based system': each procedurally generated recruit has eight attributes, a background (farmer, thief, hedge knight...) that shapes stats/cost/events, traits, and a perk tree chosen on level-up.
WHY: Backgrounds + traits + perks make every recruit a build puzzle, so roster construction itself is gameplay and no two companies feel alike.

## Granular equipment/loadout system
'Extremely granular approach to equipment introduces reason to vary your formations and loadouts depending on the team you've assembled' — weapon types have distinct skills; armor/helmet durability matters. Beasts & Exploration added crafting from monster parts and armor attachments.
WHY: Gear is a parallel progression track and tactical lever (counter-picking loadouts per enemy faction), reinforcing the quartermaster fantasy.

## Origins/starting scenarios (DLC)
Warriors of the North added 'different starting scenarios' for the company; Of Flesh and Faith (free) added two 'highly detailed origins — the Oathtakers and the Anatomists' with unique rules, banners, equipment, and 50+ events.
WHY: Origins are a cheap-to-build, high-replay-value system: they re-flavor the whole sandbox with new constraints/goals without new core mechanics.

## Legendary locations and boss content (DLC)
Lindwurm (free) added a legendary adversary guarding a treasure hoard; Beasts & Exploration added a larger world with hidden legendary locations, new beasts, contracts, and events; Blazing Deserts added a southern region with city-states, arena combat, desert raiders, and Arabian/Persian-inspired gear.
WHY: Optional super-hard landmark fights give the sandbox aspirational endgame goals and reward exploration.

- Symmetric rules ('enemies use identical weapons, skills, and rules as players') are repeatedly praised as 'fair' — in an RNG-heavy permadeath game, perceived fairness is what keeps losses feeling like lessons instead of theft.
- Visible hit-percentage RNG is the top complaint ('pure anger, rage, and death' over miss streaks at high displayed odds). If you show percentages, consider streak mitigation or framing, because raw dice plus permadeath generates rage reviews even in an 89%-positive game.
- Steep learning curve and 'lack of tutorial' is a recurring complaint — a systems-dense sandbox needs better onboarding than Battle Brothers shipped with; even beginner mode 'demands patience.'
- Contract/mission variety is a weak point ('contracts type are a little limited', 'repetitive') — procedural worlds need a deep mission-template pool or the mid-game loop wears thin; DLC kept adding contracts/events for this reason.
- Permadeath + persistent injuries + procedurally generated individual mercenaries (named, with backgrounds and traits) is the emotional core — players cite attachment and loss as why they play 350+ hours; invest in making units feel like people.
- Origins/starting scenarios are extremely cost-effective replayability: Of Flesh and Faith shipped two origins, banners, and 50+ events as a free DLC and re-energized the player base 5 years post-launch.
- DLC strategy: alternate paid regional expansions (new region + faction + crisis + gear theme: North/barbarians, South/desert city-states) with free content drops (Lindwurm, Of Flesh and Faith) and cosmetic 'support the devs' banner DLCs — a long-tail model that kept reviews Very Positive.
- Dynamic world crises give a sandbox an endgame arc without scripted story — 'radically different strategies' late-game prevents the campaign from plateauing.
- Fatigue + morale + injuries layered on top of basic HP combat is what creates tactical depth; each is simple alone but they interact (heavy armor drains fatigue, deaths break morale).
- Scope discipline: a 2D, OpenGL 3.3 game needing only 2 GB RAM/2 GB storage delivered hundreds of hours of acclaimed gameplay — depth came from systems, not engine tech; this kept a small team (Overhype Studios) able to support it for years.
- Recent reviews (82%) trail lifetime (89%) — even beloved finished games drift down as expectations rise; plan for how the game reads to a new player years later (onboarding, QoL).
- Hex grid with height advantage and obstacles, rather than open movement, made positioning legible and is consistently praised — 'simple, fun and there's a lot of strategy involved.'

## PC Gamer review digest

SUMMARY: PC Gamer's Battle Brothers review (by Ian Birnbaum, scored 84/100, verdict: "Don't let some clunky inventories scare you off from this excellent strategy RPG") portrays a "SimMercenary" medieval strategy RPG whose magic comes from an open-ended, low-stakes contract loop — march anywhere you can afford food for, hire anyone you can afford, take a job, get paid, keep the company alive — fused with brutal X-COM-style hex tactics and permadeath ("Game of Thrones has a one-night stand with X-COM"). Praise centers on the freeing world map (compared to Mount & Blade and Firefly), the "cleverly constructed, carefully balanced board game" of weapon trade-offs, the X-COM-grade emotional swings (a wondrous 19% headshot; a veteran "suddenly gutted, lost forever behind the veil of permadeath"), and the shockingly brutal pixel-art gore atop cute bobblehead soldiers; criticism targets the total absence of in-game tutorials (the reviewer wasted turns not understanding Zone of Control and only discovered the formation designer by accident hours in) and clunky inventory/shopping menus so bad he kept a paper notepad beside the keyboard to compare weapons. Memorable anecdotes include hiring "a drunk, a farmhand, and an elderly shepherd with a club foot" because good fighters were unaffordable, a contract paying 400 crowns that barely broke even after chartering a boat and marching a week, and a bandit ambush that left three men dead and the company "exactly where we started: six men, no experience, no equipment. We take another job."

## Open world-map mercenary sandbox
A strategic overworld of villages, castles, roads, mountains and wilderness (a 'quaint German countryside'). The whole company is represented by one little mercenary figure; clicking any settlement starts it marching. AI army patrols and trade caravans move on roads; bandits and monsters ('things that growl in the dark') occupy the spaces between. Movement is gated only by supplies: 'You can march anywhere as long as you've got enough food to get there. You can hire anyone as long as you've got the coin.'
WHY: Creates a 'beautifully freeing' Mount & Blade / Firefly fantasy — take a job, don't matter what it is, get paid, protect your crew, keep marching. Freedom comes from resource constraints rather than map gating.

## Contract economy with razor-thin margins
Settlements offer paid contracts (e.g., 400 crowns to kill bandits), but quest-givers omit hidden costs — the reviewer had to charter a boat and march a week to reach targets, nearly erasing profit. Income is consumed by food, gear, healing supplies, and new hires. Crucially the game is balanced so profits are a means to an end (funding the next job), not wealth accumulation: 'You're not building a retirement plan, here, so breaking even is OK.'
WHY: Money pressure transforms the player's persona — 'In combat, I'm aggressive and decisive. But when the fighting stops, I turn into a miser. Every coin and every bandage is money we need to stay alive.' Economic survival, not victory, is the campaign through-line.

## Recruit market with quality/price tiers and flavorful backgrounds
Taverns hold pools of potential recruits at varying quality and cost. Backgrounds telegraph quality: good solid fighting men cost more than the player can afford early, so the reviewer instead hired 'a drunk, a farmhand, and an elderly shepherd with a club foot,' gave them swords, and went to war.
WHY: Forces interesting compromises under poverty and generates emergent characters/stories; cheap flawed recruits make later veterans feel earned.

## Counter-based weapon design ('balanced board game')
Each weapon type has special abilities and an explicit niche with counters: maces are weak but good against armor; heavy axes are very powerful but slow. Outfitting the roster is described as a deep strategic exercise where 'every choice... is an exercise in trade-offs, in choosing the least-bad option.'
WHY: No dominant strategy — depth comes from rock-paper-scissors trade-offs in loadout decisions before battle, not just tactics during it.

## Hex-based turn-based tactical combat
On contact, the map screen 'dissolves into a hex-based battlefield dotted with trees and stones.' Both sides alternate unit turns of movement and attacks, with X-COM-style hit percentages (e.g., a 19% headshot). Soldiers are bobblehead pixel-art figures that hop between hexes 'as if counting spaces out loud in Monopoly.'
WHY: Familiar X-COM-like tactical core delivering the same 'victory and despair' swings, reskinned for low-fantasy medieval mercenary work.

## Zone of Control / engagement lock
Every character projects a one-hex boundary; once an enemy enters it you cannot freely disengage from melee — attempting to move away costs damage and wastes the turn. The reviewer learned this the hard way when a goblin rushed his archer and his retreat attempts kept failing.
WHY: Makes positioning and screening (front-line spearmen protecting rear archers) the heart of tactics — but it was completely untaught, a cited frustration.

## Pre-battle formation designer in the company management screen
A management screen lets you design a default battle formation (archers in the rear, armored spearmen in the center) so you don't manually re-order troops at the start of every fight. Roster caps at 20 soldiers, each with an individual loadout screen and skills that level up.
WHY: Removes repetitive setup — but the reviewer only discovered it 'accidentally' after several hours, an example of poor feature discoverability.

## Graphic injury and gore system on cute pixel art
Specific brutal outcomes are depicted: a spearman's neck exploding from an axe blow with the head 'rolling in a shower of arterial blood,' punctured lungs, men slowly beaten to death with clubs 'eyes going black with blood,' a dog's head sewn onto a corpse.
WHY: The tonal whiplash between adorable bobbleheads and savage violence ('Game of Thrones has a one-night stand with X-COM') gives combat real weight and identity.

## Permadeath and post-battle attrition loop
Dead soldiers are gone forever. After battle you loot corpses for 'meager, broken gear,' return to get paid, then spend earnings on healing and rearming. After one bad ambush the reviewer lost three men and ended 'exactly where we started: six men, no experience, no equipment. We take another job.'
WHY: Permadeath plus economic attrition creates the X-COM emotional register — the wondrous 19% headshot, the dodging swordsman, the gutted veteran — and makes mere survival feel like progress.

- Teach systems in-game: the review's biggest functional complaint is zero tutorials for 'a complicated board game with a lot of rules' — the reviewer wasted turns not understanding Zone of Control and only found the formation editor by accident hours in. His line 'I shouldn't have to watch 40 minutes of YouTube voiceover before I'm ready to play' is the cautionary quote; don't outsource onboarding to community videos.
- Invest in comparison UX for gear and recruits: comparing a shop weapon against the company's current loadouts required closing screens, opening sub-menus, and clicking through individual soldiers — the reviewer literally kept a paper notepad by the keyboard. Build side-by-side item comparison and a roster-wide equipment overview; clunky inventories were the one flaw called out in the final verdict.
- Make the economy a pressure system, not a profit system: balancing so the player roughly breaks even ('You're not building a retirement plan... breaking even is OK') turns money into dramatic tension — every coin and bandage matters — and reshapes player behavior (aggressive in combat, miserly between fights).
- Low stakes can be as rewarding as world-saving stakes: 'I don't have to save the world, I just have to make enough to fix my gear, hire a new sword, and go on to my next contract... it feels just as rewarding.' A tight survival loop substitutes for epic narrative.
- Hidden costs create stories: a 400-crown contract that secretly required chartering a boat and a week's march produced the review's opening anecdote. Letting quest-givers under-disclose costs generates memorable friction (use sparingly).
- Design weapons as explicit trade-offs with counters (mace weak but anti-armor, heavy axe strong but slow) so every loadout decision is 'choosing the least-bad option' — the reviewer credits this 'cleverly constructed, carefully balanced board game' structure for strategic depth.
- Permadeath plus flavorful low-tier recruits (the drunk, the farmhand, the club-footed shepherd) is an emergent-story engine: cheap flawed hires make survivors into characters, and losses ('the veteran soldier suddenly gutted') land emotionally like X-COM deaths.
- Tonal contrast amplifies impact: pairing adorable bobblehead pixel art with the most brutal gore the reviewer had ever seen in pixel art gives the game a distinct identity and makes combat consequences visceral.
- UI friction is survivable but caps the score: the core loop was 'well-oiled and dependably fun' and earned an 84, with menus the explicit grumble — strong systems can carry weak UI, but UI is what reviewers name when docking points.
- Default automation for repeated setup (saved battle formations) is essential in a game with many battles — and must be surfaced prominently, since burying it cost the player hours of manual troop re-ordering every fight.

## Rock Paper Shotgun digest

SUMMARY: Two RPS pieces by Sin Vega — "Have You Played... Battle Brothers?" (2020-06-23) and the deeper retrospective "The Rally Point: The complete Battle Brothers is still the mercenary game to beat" (2023-02-02) — frame Battle Brothers as a very difficult, tightly focused mercenary-company management/tactics game whose power comes from attachment to named, flawed recruits and from grounding contracts and random events in real simulated map conditions. The writer praises its refusal to dilute its core concept (contrasting Bannerlord and shallow "do everything" open worlds), its weapons-as-tools combat, and DLC that interlocks to deepen rather than broaden, while admitting she remains "terrible" at it, still struggles to fully enjoy it, and notes its narratives vary little beyond winning or losing repetitive fights.

## Mercenary company management over a procedural strategic map
A generated 2D map is dotted with towns, villages, and hidden wilderness. The player leads a party of low-fantasy medieval mercenaries around it in real-ish passing time, continuously consuming food, daily wages, and supplies for repairing armour/weapons, reloading ammo, and dressing wounds. Other AI parties (bandits, monsters, caravans, noble armies) move on the same map; bumping into a hostile one triggers a tactical battle.
WHY: The constant economic drain forces the player to keep taking work and to weigh every fight as a profit/loss decision ('Are we getting paid enough?'), creating the fantasy of being a working mercenary outfit rather than an adventuring party.

## Hex-grid tactical combat with individual initiative
Battles play on a hex grid where units act in initiative order — fastest characters move first, explicitly NOT XCOM-style alternating team turns — until one side is dead or has fled.
WHY: Interleaved initiative makes speed a meaningful stat and makes fights feel like chaotic melees rather than chess-like volley exchanges, distinguishing it from mainstream tactics games.

## Contracts grounded in live world simulation
Each town belongs to a noble house and offers 1-2 contracts every few days (kill bandits/monsters, escort caravans). Contracts are backed by real map state: those bandits really are raiding caravans, which deprives the town of supplies and drives prices up; monsters scaring locals reduces the pool of recruits who venture out to sign up. Players can negotiate total pay or an upfront portion, or decline.
WHY: Bread-and-butter jobs become compelling because the fiction is verifiable in the simulation — the world visibly degrades when problems go unsolved, so contracts feel like real work, not quest-board filler.

## Incomplete-information contract pricing and risk
Contracts carry only loose difficulty ratings. Higher pay often (but not always) signals a rougher ride; caravan escorts can be overpaid windfalls (clients pay 'just in case', and escorts feed your whole company en route), while a cheap 'easy' bandit camp can secretly be backed by a necromancer. Backing out of an accepted deal costs hard-earned reputation.
WHY: Every job is a gamble with asymmetric information, producing tension, judgment calls about when to cut losses, and memorable surprises — the game is fundamentally about mitigating risk (the writer compares it to Blood Bowl).

## Lethal, fatigue-driven combat
Combat is 'grubby, risky, and unglamorous': any rando with a knife can decapitate a veteran in one lucky swipe; there is no resurrection. Fighters accumulate fatigue each action, worse in heavy armour, so even the most skilled tire if not paced. Enemies exploit this — a necromancer keeps reanimating untiring zombies until your men puff out.
WHY: Permanent, swingy lethality plus a stamina economy makes retreat a core skill ('know when to say no'), keeps even trash fights tense, and turns enemy design into counterplay against player systems rather than just bigger numbers.

## Weapons as tools, not stat sticks
Weapons have stats, but more importantly each enables distinct attack types (the writer bizarrely but aptly compares this to Dark Souls). Spears are accurate but weak, yet 'spearwall' lets you control where enemies can move; a humble meat cleaver beats an expensive sword against undead because it has an explicit decapitate attack. Choice weighs damage vs utility vs the specific brother's skills.
WHY: Avoids the Sword+2 vs Sword+3 trap — equipment choices become tactical decisions 'in the grim way of real warfare', and cheap gear stays relevant.

## Named recruits with backgrounds, traits, and hidden potential
Every recruit is named and statted with a professional background (soldier, hunter, labourer, scholar, beggar, jester, wild man...), often personal traits, and varying hidden potential across stats (melee, ranged, defence, endurance, speed, resolve). Veterans cost thousands of gold upfront; the downtrodden are cheap but can surprise you with hidden talent or grow into top fighters if they survive (e.g., Ejnar, a hired 'cripple' kept as camp company, who later became one of the company's best warriors).
WHY: Creates the hiring meta-game (expensive certainty vs cheap lottery tickets) and the emotional core: players project personalities onto recruits and build headcanon (Hilmar the jester kept his unprotective jingling hat on the front line).

## Permanent injuries, scars, and trauma
Survivors accumulate debilitating, ever-mounting physical and emotional scars, including permanent disabling injuries; characters can suffer trauma from killing for a living ('screaming themselves awake').
WHY: Losses are graded, not binary — a survivor can still be diminished forever — deepening attachment and the cost calculus of every fight.

## Contextual semi-random event system
Micro-story events pop up unpredictably, conditioned on map location, current enemies, recent party actions, how the last battle went, time of day, reputation, supplies, and member backgrounds. Examples: a punch-up between your southerners and northerners; a peasant brawling with your anatomist over a book he carries; a non-combatant cripple comforting an injured brother to ease his trauma; a wild man unlocking a special option to free another caged wild man (who then tears the merchant apart, netting two wild men, a dog, and a falcon); the game repeatedly offering to let you stop a jester's dangerous stunts.
WHY: Backgrounds and world state pay off narratively, generating personal micro-stories that make a mechanically repetitive loop feel alive and individual ('not the greatest warriors, but they are brothers').

## Party-over-individual survival philosophy (no legacy)
There is no legacy system, no inheriting children (contrast Wildermyth): the company itself is the persistent entity, and its survival overrides the value of any one member. Sometimes even your favourite, most levelled brother takes an arrow to the neck and you accept it; sometimes you must accept a fight is lost and disband/lose the party entirely.
WHY: Produces a distinctive tension between attachment to slowly-levelled individuals and cold company-first pragmatism — 'the attitude of the knife' — teaching players to lose.

## Late-game crises and replay structure
Campaigns build toward 'late game crises' (DLC added a new one) that the company survives or doesn't. Ironman mode is recommended but not mandated, and lower difficulties exist; the game is explicitly built to be replayed to encounter new permutations (e.g., ifrit attacking nomads you were hired to oust becoming a three-way fight, then four-way when the dead rose to attack everyone).
WHY: Replays expose combinatorial variety rather than a fixed story; opt-in ironman respects players who would be 'killed stone dead' by mandatory permadeath campaigns.

## DLC as interlocking depth, with alternate company origins
Expansions added a bigger map, many more weapons/armours, new monsters with atypical fight structures, a new endgame crisis, and alternate starts replacing the default 'getting the band back together' (e.g., raiders who begin having alienated 80% of the map's factions, or graverobbers brewing experimental concoctions from monster parts). Each DLC takes advantage of prior ones, so their details 'bump into each other'.
WHY: Expansions variegate and combine with the core rather than bolting on side modes — the complete set forms 'a different and better whole', to the point that playing without them feels incomplete.

- Resist dilution of the core concept: RPS credits Battle Brothers' success to 'sticking resolutely to its guns', explicitly contrasting Mount & Blade II: Bannerlord (which 'faltered by throwing extraneous stuff unrelated to the core combat') and shallow do-everything open-world games. Add depth and permutations to the one loop, not breadth.
- Ground quests in simulation: contracts land emotionally because the bandits really do raid caravans and prices really do rise; players can verify the fiction. Avoid quest-board text that has no footprint in the world.
- Use incomplete information as a core mechanic: loosely-rated contract difficulty, occasional overpayment, and hidden escalations (the necromancer behind the 'easy' camp) create risk-management gameplay and stories; perfect information would flatten it.
- Make weapons tools, not incremental stat upgrades: distinct per-weapon attack verbs (spearwall, cleaver decapitation) keep cheap gear relevant and make loadout a tactical decision rather than a numbers comparison.
- Cheap, flawed recruits are an emotional goldmine: hidden potential plus background-driven events makes hiring beggars, cripples, and jesters compelling — players grow attached precisely to the underdogs (Ejnar, Hilmar, Hanke), so let weak characters surprise and let backgrounds unlock unique event options.
- Make survival graded, not binary: permanent injuries, mounting scars, and trauma — and events that mitigate them (a comrade comforting the injured) — give losses texture and consequences beyond death.
- Design enemies as counterplay to player systems: zombies are scary because reanimation attrits the fatigue economy, not because of big stats.
- Balance attachment against pragmatism deliberately: no-legacy, party-first design ('the attitude of the knife') creates a distinctive feeling — players must learn to lose, including losing whole campaigns — but recommend rather than mandate ironman, or you'll drive away players like the reviewer.
- Tone matters: be grim without being 'edgy or grimdark' — Battle Brothers stays oddly upbeat and humorous, accepts mercenary (jerk) play as valid, and sometimes makes ruthlessness the kind choice.
- Make DLC interlock: each expansion built on previous ones so details combine (alternate origins, new fight structures, crises), making the whole greater — versus standalone side-content DLC.
- Pain points to avoid: the game is brutally difficult and 'incredibly unforgiving of minor mistakes' (an RPS diary headline), with a steep multi-year mastery curve — the reviewer 'remains kind of terrible' at it and 'still struggles to fully enjoy it'. Its macro-narratives 'vary little from winning or losing repetitive fights' and surviving a late-game crisis or not — repetitive contract/fight structure is the main monotony risk, papered over by contextual events; a similar game may want stronger long-arc narrative variety.
- Individual-initiative turn order (fastest first, not team-by-team like XCOM) is a cheap way to differentiate feel and make speed/positioning stats matter.
